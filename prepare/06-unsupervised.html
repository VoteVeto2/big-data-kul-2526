The landscape: what makes learning "unsupervised"

Restate the problem: in one or two sentences, what is the defining difference between supervised and unsupervised learning — and what is the catch in the word "unsupervised"?

Supervised learning has a labelled target and correlates features to it (predict the future: classification for categorical targets, regression for continuous). Unsupervised learning has no target/label and instead describes patterns in the data. The catch: although you supply no labels, you still must define what kind of pattern you want — clusters? association rules? anomalies? The algorithm cannot decide that for you.

The problem — describing structure without a teacher

Most of the course so far chased a target: a known \( y \) you correlate features to. But enormous amounts of data arrive without any labels — supermarket baskets, gene-expression matrices, customer logs. Nobody hand-labelled "this customer is a Gold segment." The problem: how do you extract usable structure when there is no answer key to optimize against?

This lecture covers the four families that answer that question:

Clustering
Group objects so members of a group are more similar to each other than to members of other groups.
Dimensionality reduction
Project high-dimensional data into a lower-dimensional space (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) for visualization, preprocessing, or downstream models.
Anomaly detection
Identify instances that do not conform to the expected pattern (fraud, defects, errors).
Association rules
Discover "interesting" relations between items (market-basket analysis, apriori).

The solution — define the pattern, then mine it

Because there is no \( y \) to anchor optimization, every unsupervised method makes you commit to a notion of "interesting structure" — a distance, a density, a similarity, an interestingness measure. The framing quote captures it exactly: "we don't assume a label or target … but we do need to define what kind of patterns we want."

Drill the why — why isn't unsupervised "assumption-free"?

Without a label, the objective is not handed to you, so you supply the inductive bias. K-means assumes "good = compact round clusters." DBSCAN assumes "good = dense regions." Apriori assumes "interesting = frequent co-occurrence." Change that assumption and the "natural" clusters change. The slide-6 toy grid (blobs, two-moons, concentric circles) makes the point visceral: the same data gets very different colorings depending on which pattern definition the algorithm encodes — and some algorithms simply cannot recover moons or rings.

Broader context — where these outputs go

Why it matters

Unsupervised outputs are rarely the end. Clustering can be a first step toward prediction (mine a decision tree on cluster labels; use labels and distances as features). Dimensionality reduction feeds visualization, anomaly detection, and downstream models. Anomaly detection reuses earlier material ((V)AEs, one-class SVMs, random forests, k-NN). Association-rule confidence is just a conditional probability \( P(Y \mid X) \), and can be made cost-/profit-sensitive. The whole chapter is a toolbox for the "describe / explore first" phase of analytics.

Clustering — the two big families

Restate: what are the two top-level types of clustering, and what fundamentally distinguishes them in their output?

Hierarchical clustering produces a whole nested hierarchy (a dendrogram) — many partitions at once, at every granularity. Partitional clustering produces one flat partition into \( k \) non-overlapping clusters, chosen by optimizing an objective function (e.g. K-means). Key consequence: hierarchical does not need \( k \) up front (you cut the tree later); partitional does.

The problem — "more similar to each other than to others" is underspecified

The definition of a cluster ("objects in the same group are more similar to each other than to those in other groups") hides two decisions you must make before any algorithm runs: how do I measure similarity between two points, and how do I measure similarity between two groups? The two families resolve the "how do I build the grouping" question differently.

Hierarchical clustering
Build a hierarchical decomposition using a connectivity/distance criterion. Agglomerative (bottom-up): start with singletons, aggregate. Divisive (top-down): start with the whole set, divide into partitions.
Partitional clustering
Objective-function based: construct partitions and score them by a criterion. Examples: K-means, K-means++.
Exam trap — don't swap the directions

Divisive = top-down (whole set → partitions → singletons). Agglomerative = bottom-up (singletons → … → one big cluster). The slide-8 diagram is literally the same binary tree read in opposite directions. And in practice: all real implementations are agglomerative, because divisive must consider all possible ways to split a cluster (way more expensive).

The mechanism — distance metric vs linkage criterion

To decide which clusters to merge (or split), hierarchical clustering needs a measure of (dis)similarity between sets of observations. This is built from two distinct ingredients — keep them separate in your head:

  1. Distance metric — distance between a pair of observations (Euclidean, Manhattan, …).
  2. Linkage criterion — dissimilarity of two sets, expressed as a function of the pairwise distances within/between them (single, complete, average, centroid, Ward's).

Distance metrics (point-to-point)

Euclidean (L2)\[ \lVert a-b \rVert_2 = \sqrt{\sum_i (a_i - b_i)^2} \]
Squared Euclidean (L2²)\[ \lVert a-b \rVert_2^2 = \sum_i (a_i - b_i)^2 \]
Manhattan (L1)\[ \lVert a-b \rVert_1 = \sum_i \lvert a_i - b_i \rvert \]
Maximum / Chebyshev (L∞)\[ \lVert a-b \rVert_\infty = \max_i \lvert a_i - b_i \rvert \]

A function deserves to be called a metric only if it satisfies four axioms:

  • Symmetry: \( d(a,b) = d(b,a) \)
  • Constancy of self-similarity: \( d(a,a) = 0 \)
  • Positivity: \( d(a,b) \ge 0 \)
  • Triangular inequality: \( d(a,b) \le d(a,c) + d(c,b) \)
Exam subtlety — squared Euclidean

Squared Euclidean is used everywhere (it is exactly the SSE term in K-means), but it violates the triangle inequality, so strictly it is not a true metric — even though the slides list it among the "distances." If asked "which of these is/are valid metrics," remember the four axioms are what a metric should possess.

Linkage criteria (set-to-set)

Single (minimum) linkage\[ D(A,B) = \min\{\, d(a,b) : a \in A,\, b \in B \,\} \]
Complete (maximum) linkage\[ D(A,B) = \max\{\, d(a,b) : a \in A,\, b \in B \,\} \]
Average (mean) linkage\[ D(A,B) = \frac{1}{\lvert A \rvert \, \lvert B \rvert} \sum_{a \in A}\sum_{b \in B} d(a,b) \]
Centroid linkage\[ D(A,B) = d(a_c,\, b_c) \quad (a_c,\,b_c \text{ are cluster centroids}) \]
Ward's linkage\[ D(A,B) = \mathrm{SSE}(A \cup B) - \big(\mathrm{SSE}(A) + \mathrm{SSE}(B)\big) \]
LinkageUsesGeometric effect
Single (min)closest pairLong, skinny clusters — chaining
Complete (max)farthest pairTight, compact clusters
Average (mean)all pairs averagedFavorable in most cases, robust to noise
Centroidcentroid distanceRobust, but needs a centroid concept
Ward'sincrease in SSEANOVA-like; merges that least inflate within-cluster variance
Drill the why — why does single linkage "chain"?

Single linkage scores two clusters by their single closest pair. So one lonely bridge point sitting between two blobs is enough to glue them, regardless of how far apart everything else is. The result is stringy, snake-like clusters that follow a thin trail of points. Complete linkage does the opposite — it scores by the farthest pair, so a merge only happens when every pair is reasonably close, forcing compact balls. Ward's asks a different question entirely: "by how much does the within-cluster sum of squared distances grow if I merge?" — and merges the pair that grows it least, hence the ANOVA analogy (variance-based, but on distances, not target-vs-prediction error).

Dendrograms, trade-offs, and outliers

  • No need to specify \( k \) in advance — you make a horizontal cut in the dendrogram; the number of branches it crosses = number of clusters.
  • Maps nicely to human intuition in some domains; but not very scalable and local optima are a problem (the hierarchy isn't necessarily optimal).
  • Normalize features first.
  • A dendrogram is a good outlier indicator: instances that join very late / at large heights are candidate anomalies.

K-means — the workhorse partitional method

Restate the problem K-means solves, and sketch its loop from memory before reading on.

K-means partitions \( n \) points into exactly \( k \) non-overlapping clusters so that points are close to their cluster's centre. Loop: (1) choose \( k \); (2) initialize \( k \) centres; (3) assign each point to its nearest centre; (4) recompute each centre as the mean of its assigned points; (5) repeat 3–4 until no point changes cluster. It minimizes the total squared distance to centroids (SSE / distortion).

The problem — a fast, flat partition with a clear objective

Hierarchical clustering is intuitive but slow and gives you a whole tree you then have to cut. Often you just want one flat partition into \( k \) groups, computed cheaply, optimizing an explicit notion of "good." That notion: high intra-cluster similarity, low inter-cluster similarity.

The solution — assign / update until stable

  1. Decide a value for \( k \).
  2. Initialize \( k \) cluster centres (randomly over the feature space, or by picking random instances).
  3. Assign each instance to the nearest centre under some distance measure.
  4. Recalculate the \( k \) centres (mean of assigned points).
  5. Repeat 3–4 until no object changed membership.

It minimizes the total distortion — the sum over clusters of squared distances from points to their centroid:

K-means objective (SSE / distortion)\[ \min \; \mathrm{SSE} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \sum_{i=1}^{n_k} \lVert x_{ki} - \mu_k \rVert^2 \]

where \( \mu_k \) is the centroid of cluster \( k \) and \( n_k \) its size.

Drill the why — why is K-means only a "heuristic"?

Exactly minimizing that SSE over all assignments is computationally hard (combinatorial). The assign/update loop is guaranteed to decrease SSE every iteration and converge, but only to a local optimum that depends on the initial centres — not necessarily the global best. The lone exception noted in the slides: in the one-dimensional case it can be solved exactly. This is why initialization matters so much (random restarts, K-means++).

Strengths, weaknesses, and the shapes it cannot see

Strengths

  • Very simple to implement and debug.
  • Intuitive objective (optimizes intra-cluster similarity).
  • Relatively efficient.

Weaknesses

  • Needs the mean defined → trouble with categorical data.
  • Lands in local optima → init is critical.
  • Must specify \( k \) (use the elbow method).
  • Sensitive to noise/outliers.
  • Normalization often required.
  • Cannot find non-convex shapes or unequal densities.
ELI-intern — why K-means fails on two-moons

Picture each cluster as a round balloon centred on its mean. K-means assigns every point to whichever balloon's centre is nearest, so the boundaries between clusters are always straight lines halfway between centres (a Voronoi diagram) — the clusters come out convex and roughly round. The two-moons dataset is two interlocking crescents: the "right" answer is curved and non-convex, which round balloons simply cannot trace. Same story with different densities — K-means splits by distance to centre, blind to how tightly packed each blob is, so it carves a dense blob and a sparse blob in the wrong place. If you need curved or density-based shapes, reach for DBSCAN or mean-shift.

Choosing \( k \) — the elbow method

Plot the objective \( J \) (SSE) against \( k \). As \( k \) grows, SSE keeps dropping (more centres fit tighter), but at some point extra clusters stop helping much — the curve bends. Pick that "elbow"/knee. The Python package kneed can detect it automatically.

Practical wisdom

Don't be too swayed by intuition about the "right" \( k \). Often it pays to start with a higher \( k \) and post-process: the slide example shows the same customer data as \( k=3 \) vs \( k=5 \), where \( k=5 \) reveals extra segments (Lead, Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum) that \( k=3 \) hid.

K-means++ — initialize smarter, not luckier

Random init often lands two centres inside the same true cluster, dooming the run to a bad local optimum. K-means++ spreads the initial centres apart:

  1. Choose the first centre uniformly at random.
  2. For each candidate point \( x \), compute \( d(x,c) \) = distance to the nearest already-chosen centre \( c \).
  3. Sample the next centre with probability proportional to \( d(x,c)^2 \).
  4. Repeat 2–3 until \( k \) centres are chosen, then run ordinary K-means.
K-means++ sampling probability\[ P(x \text{ chosen}) \;\propto\; d(x,c)^2 \]
Drill the why — why squared distance, and why does it help?

Squaring sharply biases the draw toward points that are far from every existing centre — exactly the under-covered regions. A point twice as far is four times as likely to be picked. So new centres tend to land in genuinely different clusters, lowering inter-cluster similarity at initialization and giving the assign/update loop a much better starting basin. It is not a different algorithm — just a smarter step 2 — and most K-means libraries use it by default.

Beyond K-means — DBSCAN, EM/GMM, mean-shift

DBSCAN — density instead of distance-to-centre

Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise. It groups points that are closely packed (many nearby neighbours) and marks isolated low-density points as noise. Two parameters: Eps (neighbourhood radius) and MinPts (minimum neighbours, e.g. 4).

Core point
Has \( \ge \) MinPts points within Eps.
Border point
Within Eps of a core point, but not itself core.
Noise
Neither core nor border — isolated → flagged as outlier.
Drill the why — why can DBSCAN do moons when K-means can't?

DBSCAN never reasons about a single centre or a round balloon. It grows clusters by connectivity through dense regions: any chain of core points within Eps of each other belongs to one cluster, however curved or non-convex that chain is. So crescents and rings are recovered naturally, and it doesn't need \( k \) — the number of clusters falls out of the density structure. The price: you must pick Eps/MinPts, and clusters of very different densities are tricky (hence HDBSCAN, the hierarchical version).

EM / GMM clustering — soft assignments

Expectation-Maximization clustering fits a mixture of Gaussians. Where K-means makes hard assignments (each point belongs fully to one cluster), EM makes soft ones (each point has a probability of membership in each cluster).

  • E-step: assign probabilistic membership — \( P(\text{cluster} \mid \text{instance}) \).
  • M-step: re-estimate the model parameters (the Gaussians' means/variances/weights) given those memberships.

Because EM learns a model per cluster, you can generate new data points from it; it extends to other model types (multinomial for categorical data, noise-robust models). But it is still a local optimization, still needs \( k \), and still struggles with non-convex shapes — "a less hard version of K-means."

ELI-intern — hard vs soft, and why "generative"

K-means files each point into exactly one folder. EM instead says "70% folder A, 30% folder B" — a soft, probabilistic split. Why bother? Because EM doesn't just remember centres; it learns a full shape per cluster (a Gaussian: a mean and a spread). Once you have the shape, you can sample brand-new points that look like they belong — that's what "generative" means. (You can fake this with K-means too: drop a Gaussian on each centroid with variance from the min-SSE, but EM does it natively.)

Mean-shift — climbing a density surface

Mean-shift is built on kernel density estimation (KDE). Assume the data was sampled from some probability distribution; KDE estimates it by placing a kernel (usually Gaussian) on every point and summing them into a smooth density surface. Points then shift toward the nearest mode (peak); each peak's basin of attraction is a cluster.

Drill the why — what does the bandwidth control, and why is it the key knob?

The bandwidth is the width of each kernel. Narrow bandwidth → a bumpy surface with many sharp peaks → many small clusters. Wide bandwidth → an over-smoothed surface with few broad peaks → few large clusters. So bandwidth implicitly sets the number of clusters — which is why mean-shift does not require you to specify \( k \), but is highly sensitive to this one parameter.

Validating clusters & adding domain knowledge

The problem — there is no ground truth to score against

Without labels, "is this clustering good?" has no objective answer. Internal validity indices stand in: they reward high within-cluster similarity and low between-cluster similarity — the same intuition K-means optimizes.

Davies–Bouldin Index

Davies–Bouldin (lower is better)\[ \mathrm{DBI} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} R_i, \qquad R_i = \max_{j \neq i} R_{ij}, \qquad R_{ij} = \frac{S_i + S_j}{D_{ij}} \]
  • \( S_i \): mean distance between cluster \( i \)'s instances and its centroid — within-cluster spread.
  • \( D_{ij} \): distance between centroids of clusters \( i \) and \( j \) — between-cluster separation.
Drill the why — why is LOW good?

Read \( R_{ij} = \frac{S_i + S_j}{D_{ij}} \) as "spread over separation." A clustering is good when clusters are tight (small \( S \), numerator) and far apart (large \( D \), denominator) → small ratio. For each cluster we take its worst (max) overlap with any other cluster, then average. So a small DBI means even each cluster's nastiest neighbour is well separated — lower is better. (Validation here is still somewhat subjective given the descriptive nature of the task.)

Incorporating domain knowledge

You often know something the data doesn't show. Two clean ways to inject it without rewriting the algorithm:

  • Must-link / can't-link constraints: declare who must (or must not) share a cluster. Cheap to add, but too many constraints can make the problem infeasible.
  • Warm start: seed the algorithm with a partial solution.

Distances for text & mixed data

Most metrics so far are numerical. For text, Levenshtein (edit) distance counts the minimum deletions, insertions, substitutions to turn one string into another (e.g. rick → rcik needs 2 edits). Others: Jaccard, cosine, and Gower (handles mixed categorical + numerical data).

Dimensionality reduction — PCA

Restate: why would you ever want to throw away dimensions, and what does PCA do mechanically to choose which directions to keep?

High-dimensional data is hard to visualize, slow to model, and often redundant/noisy. PCA finds new axes — principal components — that are linear combinations of the original features, computed from the eigenvector decomposition of the covariance matrix. Components are ordered by how much variance they explain (the eigenvalue \( \lambda_j \)) and are mutually uncorrelated. Keep the top few → low-dimensional, decorrelated representation.

The problem — too many, correlated, hard-to-see dimensions

With dozens or thousands of features, plotting is impossible, models overfit, and many features are redundant (correlated). We want a compact representation that keeps the important variation.

The solution — eigen-directions of the covariance

PCA is a linear technique. Each principal component is a linear combination of original features, with coefficients given by an eigenvector of the data's covariance matrix:

Principal component j\[ \mathrm{PC}_j = e_j' X = e_{j1}X_1 + e_{j2}X_2 + \cdots + e_{jp}X_p \]

The eigenvalue \( \lambda_j \) is the variance explained by component \( j \). Components come out ordered (most variance first) and uncorrelated.

Pro

  • Powerful data reduction.
  • Principal components are uncorrelated.

Con

  • Hard to interpret (each PC is a blend of all features).
  • Linear only — cannot capture non-linear manifolds.
Why it matters

PCA's linearity is exactly its limitation: data living on a curved manifold (a Swiss roll, interlocking digits) won't unfold under a linear projection. That gap is what t-SNE and UMAP were built to fill.

t-SNE — non-linear manifold embedding

Restate the core idea of t-SNE in plain words before the mechanics: what is it trying to make the low-dimensional map preserve?

t-SNE converts pairwise distances into neighbour probabilities and then arranges points in 2D/3D so the neighbour-probability pattern matches as closely as possible. It is built to preserve local similarities — who is close to whom — not global distances. Concretely it minimizes the KL divergence between the high-dimensional similarity distribution \( P \) and the low-dimensional one \( Q \).

The problem — PCA can't unfold a manifold

A manifold is a space that locally resembles Euclidean space near each point (a curved surface looks flat up close). Real high-dim data — images, documents — often lies on such a non-linear manifold embedded in a higher-dimensional space. A linear method like PCA flattens it wrongly. We need a technique that respects local neighbourhoods even as the global shape curves. (Other manifold methods: MDS, Isomap, LLE, autoencoders.)

The solution — two distributions, then match them

Step 1 — similarities in the original high-dim space

For each point \( x_i \), put a Gaussian kernel on it and ask: how likely would \( x_i \) pick \( x_j \) as a neighbour, in proportion to density? Normalize over all other points:

Conditional neighbour probability\[ p_{j \mid i} = \frac{\exp\!\big(-\lVert x_i - x_j \rVert^2 / (2\sigma_i^2)\big)}{\sum_{k \neq i} \exp\!\big(-\lVert x_i - x_k \rVert^2 / (2\sigma_i^2)\big)} \]
Symmetrized joint (N points)\[ p_{ij} = \frac{p_{j \mid i} + p_{i \mid j}}{2N}, \qquad p_{ii} = 0 \]

The bandwidth \( \sigma_i \) is per-point, set so the perplexity of the conditional distribution hits a user-chosen value. Result: \( \sigma_i \) adapts to local density — smaller in dense regions, larger in sparse ones.

Step 2 — similarities in the low-dim map

In the 2D/3D map, measure similarities \( q_{ij} \) between mapped points using a Student t-distribution (degrees of freedom = map dimensionality − 1), which has fatter tails than a Gaussian. No perplexity here; \( q_{ii} = 0 \).

Optimization — minimize KL divergence

t-SNE loss (Kullback–Leibler divergence)\[ \mathrm{KL}(P \,\Vert\, Q) = \sum_{i \neq j} p_{ij}\,\log\frac{p_{ij}}{q_{ij}} \]

Mapped positions are found by gradient descent on this loss.

ELI-intern — why a Student-t in the map (the "crowding" fix)

When you squash high-dimensional data into 2D, there simply isn't enough room — moderately-distant points get crammed together ("crowding"). The trick: use a fat-tailed Student-t for the map distances. Fat tails mean that to reach a given low similarity \( q \), two points must sit much farther apart than a Gaussian would require. So t-SNE pushes dissimilar points out to the edges, giving clusters breathing room and crisp gaps. That mismatch — Gaussian in the original space, Student-t in the map — is deliberate, not an accident.

Drill the why — why KL divergence, and why is it asymmetric here?

\( \mathrm{KL}(P\Vert Q) = \sum p_{ij}\log\frac{p_{ij}}{q_{ij}} \) is heavily penalized when \( p_{ij} \) is large but \( q_{ij} \) is small — i.e. when two genuinely-close points are mapped far apart. It is much more forgiving of the reverse. That asymmetry is exactly the local-structure bias: t-SNE works hard to keep true neighbours together and doesn't much care if far-apart points end up at some arbitrary distance — which is precisely why distances between clusters in the plot are not meaningful.

Worked toy example (3 points on concentric rings)

Gaussian centred at \( x_i \): raw values \( p_{j\mid i} = 0.78/z \), \( p_{k\mid i} = 0.60/z \) (\( x_j \) is closer, so larger). Normalizing against all other points uses \( z = 55.62 \). The joint probabilities come out \( p_{ij} = 0.0069 \) (more similar) vs \( p_{ik} = 0.0049 \). So \( x_j \) ends up more similar to \( x_i \) than \( x_k \) does — consistent with \( x_j \) being the nearer point. (The slide notes this is just illustrative — "reducing" 2D to 2D doesn't truly make sense.)

Perplexity — the one knob that matters

  • The most important hyperparameter; sets the number of effective nearest neighbours (like \( k \) in k-NN).
  • Denser data → larger perplexity. Typical range 5–50.
  • Different perplexities can give very different maps.
Exam trap — three things a t-SNE plot does NOT tell you

(1) Cluster size is meaningless. (2) Distance between clusters is meaningless (only local structure is preserved — it's a manifold). (3) Even random data can be made to look "meaningful." (See distill.pub/2016/misread-tsne.) Also: t-SNE is NOT a clustering technique — it's dimensionality reduction. You can run k-means/DBSCAN on the map afterward, but cautiously.

The catch — t-SNE can't embed new points

Drill the why — why is t-SNE poor for predictive featurization?

t-SNE learns a non-parametric mapping: there is no explicit function from input space to the map — the map positions are just the optimized output of one gradient-descent run on this dataset. So when a new test point arrives, there is no formula to place it; you'd have to re-run everything. That makes t-SNE unsuitable as a DR step in a predictive pipeline. Fixes exist — parametric t-SNE learns a regressor (or net) that predicts map coordinates — and this is exactly the gap UMAP closes by being parametric by default.

You may also supply your own similarity matrix (diagonal 0, normalized to sum 1) or a distance matrix (similarity \( = 1 - \) distance), skipping perplexity tuning but then choosing a similarity metric yourself. Because it's gradient descent, learning rate and init matter; init is sometimes done with PCA.

9

In a t-SNE plot, cluster A is much bigger and sits far from cluster B. What can you legitimately conclude?

  1. A contains many more points / covers more of the data than B.
  2. A and B are very dissimilar because they are far apart.
  3. Only that points within a cluster were local neighbours; size and distance are not meaningful.

Which correctly matches t-SNE's two steps?

  1. Step 1: Gaussian (perplexity sets \( \sigma_i \)); Step 2: Student-t (no perplexity).
  2. Step 1: Student-t; Step 2: Gaussian, both using perplexity.
  3. Both steps use Gaussians; perplexity tunes the map distribution.

Anomaly detection

Restate: what counts as an "anomaly," and why is it usually worth detecting?

An anomaly (a.k.a. outlier / novelty) is an instance that does not conform to the expected pattern or to the rest of the dataset. They matter because they usually translate to a real problem worth flagging: bank fraud, structural defects, medical issues, data errors. Methods span unsupervised, supervised, and statistical techniques.

The problem — anomalies are rare and often unlabelled

You rarely have a clean labelled set of "frauds." Anomalies are scarce, diverse, and shift over time. So most methods learn what normal looks like and flag deviations — reusing tools from earlier chapters in clever ways.

The families — and the single idea behind each

Statistical
Histograms, box plots, distributions; or robust covariance (fit an inlier ellipse). Easy, but often univariate.
Clustering-based
Cluster, then flag who falls outside: far from any cluster, in tiny clusters, joining the dendrogram very late, or labelled noise by DBSCAN. Can be combined with DR (find anomalies in a t-SNE map).
One-class SVM
Learn a frontier around the normal/training data; points outside are novelties. Linked to PU (positive-unlabeled) learning.
Isolation forest
Random-forest-style trees with random splits; anomaly score = average root-to-leaf path length.
Local Outlier Factor (LOF)
k-NN density: compare a point's local density to its neighbours'.
(V)AE
High reconstruction error → anomaly.
CADE
Real vs synthetic-uniform classification trick.

Isolation forests — outliers are easy to isolate

Drill the why — why do short paths mean "anomaly"?

An isolation forest builds trees by splitting on random features at random thresholds (an extreme form of extremely-randomized trees — not entropy-driven like a normal random forest). A point sitting out in empty space gets fenced off after very few random cuts — it is easy to isolate. A point buried inside a dense cluster needs many cuts to separate. So the average path length from root to leaf is short for anomalies, and that short path is the anomaly score. Beautifully, you flag outliers without ever modelling "normal."

LOF — lonelier than your neighbours

Based on k-NN. LOF estimates a point's local density from its k nearest neighbours' distances, then compares it to its neighbours' local densities. A point whose density is substantially lower than its neighbours' is an outlier — locality matters, so a point can be an outlier relative to a dense region while looking fine globally.

(V)AE & CADE — reusing prediction tools

(V)AE: an autoencoder trained on normal data reconstructs normal points well and abnormal points badly, so reconstruction error is an anomaly proxy. For VAEs you can read both the distortion \( D = \mathbb{E}_{p_d, q_\phi}[-\log p_\theta(x \mid z)] \) (reconstruction term) and the rate \( R = \mathbb{E}_{p_d}[\mathrm{KL}(q_\phi(z\mid x)\,\Vert\,p(z))] \) (latent regularization) — anomalies can show up as either high reconstruction error or unusual latent regions.

Drill the why — CADE turns anomaly detection into ordinary classification

Classifier-Adjusted Density Estimation has no real outliers to learn from, so it manufactures them: label all real instances \( y = 0 \), then generate a synthetic set by sampling uniformly across the feature ranges and label them \( y = 1 \). Train any probabilistic classifier to separate real from synthetic. Real instances that the classifier thinks look like the uniform-random junk (i.e. live in sparsely-populated regions) get a high outlier score. It is a clever bootstrap: the classifier implicitly estimates where real data is dense vs sparse.

In an isolation forest, an instance has a very short average root-to-leaf path length. This means it is…

  1. a normal point deep inside a dense cluster.
  2. likely an anomaly — it was isolated with very few random splits.
  3. undetermined; path length is unrelated to anomaly score.

How does CADE create "outlier" training examples when none are labelled?

  1. It uses the points with the highest reconstruction error from a VAE.
  2. It generates synthetic instances by uniform sampling over the features and labels them \( y=1 \).
  3. It takes DBSCAN's noise points as the positive class.

Association rules — finding "interesting" co-occurrences

Restate: what is an association rule, what does a transactional database look like, and does order matter?

An association rule is an implication \( X \Rightarrow Y \) between itemsets, e.g. {onions, tomatoes, ketchup} → {burger}. The data is a transactional database: each row is a transaction, each column a binary item indicator (1 = item present). Standard association mining ignores the order of items within a transaction — that's what sequence mining (GSP) adds. Pioneering algorithm: apriori (Agrawal, 1993).

The problem — which of the exponentially many patterns are worth surfacing?

Given baskets of items, we want rules like "buys A and B ⇒ buys C." But the space of possible itemsets is the power set of all items, of size \( 2^{\lvert I \rvert} - 1 \) — astronomically large. We need both a notion of "interesting" and an algorithm that finds the interesting ones without enumerating everything.

The solution — interestingness measures

Support of itemset X\[ \mathrm{Support}(X) = \frac{\lvert \{\, t \in T : X \subseteq t \,\} \rvert}{\lvert T \rvert} \]
Confidence of rule X ⇒ Y\[ \mathrm{Confidence}(X \Rightarrow Y) = \frac{\mathrm{Support}(X \cup Y)}{\mathrm{Support}(X)} \;\approx\; P(Y \mid X) \]
Lift\[ \mathrm{Lift}(X \Rightarrow Y) = \frac{\mathrm{Support}(X \cup Y)}{\mathrm{Support}(X)\,\mathrm{Support}(Y)} \]
Conviction\[ \mathrm{Conviction}(X \Rightarrow Y) = \frac{1 - \mathrm{Support}(Y)}{1 - \mathrm{Confidence}(X \Rightarrow Y)} \]
  • Support = fraction of transactions containing the itemset (how frequent).
  • Confidence = estimate of \( P(Y \mid X) \): given \( X \), how often does \( Y \) follow.
  • Lift: \( =1 \) means \( X \) and \( Y \) are independent; \( \gt 1 \) means positive dependence.
  • Conviction: how often the rule would be wrong if \( X \) and \( Y \) were independent (1.2 = wrong 1.2× as often by chance).
Drill the why — why confidence alone can mislead, and lift fixes it

Confidence divides by \( \mathrm{Support}(X) \) only, so a rule \( X \Rightarrow Y \) can score high confidence merely because \( Y \) is popular on its own (everyone buys bread). Lift divides additionally by \( \mathrm{Support}(Y) \), comparing the joint rate to what you'd expect under independence. If buying \( X \) doesn't actually raise the chance of \( Y \) above baseline, \( \mathrm{Lift} = 1 \) and you correctly judge the rule uninteresting — even if its confidence looked impressive.

Worked: support & confidence on the basket DB

Support({milk, bread, cheese}) = 2/5 = 0.4 — only transactions 103 and 105 contain all three.

Confidence({cheese, wine} ⇒ {spaghetti}) = Support({cheese, wine, spaghetti}) / Support({cheese, wine}) = 0.4 / 0.6 = 0.66. (cheese+wine+spaghetti in 104, 105 → 0.4; cheese+wine in 102, 104, 105 → 0.6.)

Why it matters

Many more measures exist (the slide lists 21: φ-coefficient, odds ratio, Kappa, J-Measure, Gini, …), and there are cost-sensitive ("profit/utility") versions, e.g. \( \mathrm{ExpectedProfit}(X \Rightarrow Y) = \mathrm{Confidence}(X \Rightarrow Y)\cdot\sum_i \mathrm{Profit}(Y_i) \). The right measure depends on what "interesting" means for your business.

The apriori algorithm — prune the power set

Apriori is two-stage: (1) apply a minimum support threshold to find all frequent itemsets, then (2) apply a minimum confidence threshold to those to form rules. The hard part is stage 1: the candidate space is the power set of size \( 2^{\lvert I \rvert} - 1 \) (for 100 items that's 1,271,427,896 — brute force is hopeless).

Drill the why — the downward-closure property is the whole trick

Anti-monotonicity / downward closure: if an itemset is frequent, all its subsets are frequent too; contrapositively, if an itemset is infrequent, every superset is also infrequent. Why must this hold? Adding an item to a set can only shrink or keep the count of transactions containing it (it's a stricter requirement), so support never increases as itemsets grow. This lets apriori prune entire branches: the moment beer (support 0.4) falls below threshold, no itemset containing beer is ever considered again.

Join-and-prune candidate generation

To build candidate \( k \)-itemsets:

  1. Look at previous: keep only the frequent \( (k-1) \)-itemsets.
  2. Join on self: join the frequent \( (k-1) \)-itemsets with themselves to form length-\( k \) candidates.
  3. Prune: discard any candidate that has a \( (k-1) \)-subset which was not frequent.
Worked: generating 3-itemsets (≥ 50% support)

Frequent 2-itemsets: {milk,bread}, {bread,cheese}, {cheese,wine}, {cheese,spaghetti}. Joining gives candidates {milk,bread,cheese}, {bread,cheese,wine}, {bread,cheese,spaghetti}, {cheese,wine,spaghetti}. Each contains an infrequent 2-subset (e.g. {milk,cheese} 0.4, {bread,wine} 0.4, {bread,spaghetti} 0.4, {wine,spaghetti} 0.4) → all candidates are pruned. No frequent 3-itemset survives at this threshold.

From frequent itemsets to rules

Rule generation (per frequent itemset I)\[ \forall\, I_s \in P(I): I_s \neq \varnothing \,\wedge\, I_s \neq I \,\wedge\, \mathrm{Confidence}(I_s \Rightarrow I \setminus I_s) \gt \mathrm{minconf} \;\Rightarrow\; I_s \Rightarrow I \setminus I_s \]

For each frequent itemset, enumerate every non-empty proper subset \( I_s \), form the rule \( I_s \Rightarrow I \setminus I_s \), and keep those above minconf.

Apriori discovers that {milk, bread} is infrequent. What does downward closure let it conclude immediately?

  1. All subsets of {milk, bread} are also infrequent.
  2. Every superset (e.g. {milk, bread, cheese}) is also infrequent and can be pruned.
  3. Nothing — support is not monotone, so each itemset must be counted.

A rule has \( \mathrm{Lift} = 1 \). What does that tell you?

  1. \( X \) strongly predicts \( Y \) (high dependence).
  2. The rule has the maximum possible confidence.
  3. \( X \) and \( Y \) are independent — \( X \) doesn't raise \( Y \)'s baseline rate.

Extensions & post-processing

  • Categorical/continuous: dummy-code each level into a binary item; bin continuous variables first (binning needs fine-tuning or support/confidence collapse). Group many-level categoricals; drop uninteresting frequent levels (e.g. browser-chrome at 90%).
  • Hierarchical items, discriminative rules (mine toward a target like → {beer}; usable as features, CARSVM), sequence mining (order matters; GSP, SPMF library), frequent subgraph mining.
  • Tuning interestingness: rare patterns (Rolex buyers) via group-based thresholds; negative patterns (Hummer + Tesla) that are negatively correlated and infrequent yet interesting; domain knowledge to block/allow itemsets.
  • Filtering: rule explosions need post-processing — sensitivity analysis on minsup/minconf, drop trivial rules (spaghetti ⇒ spaghetti sauce), prize unexpected/actionable ones; confidence is not always best (use lift, conviction, rarity). Visualization is crucial (R arulesViz).
Exam trap — order & rarity

Standard association mining ignores item order; sequence mining (GSP) respects it. And negatively-correlated / rare patterns can be more interesting than frequent positive ones — confidence is not the be-all metric.
