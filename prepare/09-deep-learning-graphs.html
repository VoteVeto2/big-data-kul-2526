Big picture: why graphs are different

Restate the problem: a bank hands you a table of card transactions and asks "predict which cardholders are fraudsters." Why can't you just throw it into XGBoost like a normal table? What is fundamentally missing — and what does a graph give you?

A flat table treats each cardholder as an independent row. But fraud is relational: stolen cards get used at the same merchants, so fraudsters cluster. That signal lives in the connections between rows, not in any single row's columns. Graph data is "(semi-)structured but has no direct feature-vector representation" — the relationships are the data. Building the graph (who connects to whom, and why) is step one, and that choice silently shapes every later finding.

The problem and why it existed

Graphs are everywhere — hyperlinks, e-mails, citations, transactions, phone calls, social follows. The recurring course example is the credit-card ↔ merchant network where fraudsters cluster because stolen cards are spent at the same stores. Other applications: churn detection, terrorism/suspicion scoring, recommendation, epidemiology, COVID movement tracking.

The course-level tension: the same use case can be turned into many different valid networks. Should an e-mail edge be directed (A wrote to B) or undirected? Weighted by count or binary? Are "merchant" and "card" the same node type or two? Every one of these decisions propagates into your metrics, communities, and predictions.

Drill the why · why is "construct the network" the hard part?

Because there is no ground-truth graph waiting in the data. Unlike pixels (a grid is given) or text (word order is given), a graph is something you impose on raw records. If you make the wrong call (e.g. directed when undirected captures the social tie better), you don't get an error message — you get worse AUC and you may never know why. Oskarsdottir (2018) found empirically that using all edges beats only reciprocated edges, and undirected networks tended to perform best — start simple.

Construction design choices

Nodes (vertices)
The "actors": people, companies, pages. Homogeneous = all one type; heterogeneous = multiple types (products + customers). Can carry features, a single node weight (intensity of presence), or you can promote an attribute (e.g. address) into its own node type to capture shared-address interactions.
Edges (links/arcs)
The "interactions". Can be one type or many; carry features; weights are very common (vs. binary presence). Watch directionality & cardinality: directed/undirected, self-edges, reciprocal, duplicated. An edge touching more than two nodes makes it a hypergraph.
Bipartite → unipartite
Authors↔papers (two types) can be projected onto author↔author (one type). Projection edge weights can be binary, common-neighbour count, or Jaccard. Example: \( w_{A,B} = \{1,\ 2,\ 0.66\} \) for {binary, common-neighbours, Jaccard}.
Line graph
Re-encode an edge as a node, when the focus of prediction is on interactions rather than actors.

Temporal: recency-weighted summary graphs

To fold a time-sequence of graphs into one summary graph \( S_t \), weight each edge by its age \( h \):

Recency edge weight\[ f(h) = e^{-\alpha h} \]

At \( h=0 \), \( f=1 \). Larger \( \alpha \) → faster decay (recent interactions dominate). With \( \alpha=2 \), an edge is nearly weightless after about 3–4 years; with \( \alpha=0.5 \) it decays slowly.

Representations and two matrices

Adjacency matrix

node × node. Entry = 1 if an edge connects the two nodes (diagonal often blank). Tells you who-is-connected-to-whom.

Incidence matrix

node × edge. Entry = 1 if the node is an endpoint of that edge. Rows are nodes, columns are edges.

Exam trap

Do not swap them. Adjacency = node×node, incidence = node×edge. A sociogram is the visual layout (needs a layout algorithm and can reveal patterns without any prediction).

Centrality — who matters in a network?

Restate the problem: "centrality" measures node importance. But importance for what? Name at least three genuinely different ways a node could be "important," before reading on.

A node can be important because it has many connections (degree — a hub), because it can reach everyone quickly (closeness — short paths), because it sits on the routes between others (betweenness — a gatekeeper/bridge), or because it's connected to other important nodes (eigenvector/PageRank — authority). These are different questions, so they crown different "kings." That is the whole point of having four measures.

Degree centrality (local connectivity)

Degree = number of edges (can split into in-degree vs. out-degree). Normalized degree = degree ÷ max degree. A high-degree node is an active player, often a connector/hub or deal-maker.

Drill the why · high degree ≠ most "connected"

A node can have many edges that all point to low-level entities (a salesperson with 500 unimportant contacts). It looks busy but has no real reach into the important parts of the network. That gap is exactly why eigenvector centrality exists — it weights a connection by the importance of who's on the other end.

Geodesics and the graph-theoretic center

A geodesic is the shortest path between two nodes (optionally weighted). Computing all geodesics is expensive on large graphs. The graph-theoretic center is the node with the smallest worst-case distance:

Graph-theoretic center\[ \arg\min_{n\in N}\ \Big(\ \max\big(\ \{\ |\mathrm{geod}(n,m)| : m\in N\ \}\ \big)\ \Big) \]

Closeness centrality (geographic)

How near a node is to all others — the reciprocal of farness:

Closeness\[ C(x) = \frac{N-1}{\sum_{y\neq x} |\mathrm{geod}(x,y)|} \]

High closeness = quick reach and high visibility of what's happening in the network.

Betweenness centrality (transit)

The number of times a node appears on the geodesics of the network. High-betweenness nodes are gatekeepers who control information flow.

Easy-to-miss fact

Betweenness can also be computed for edges, not just nodes. This is the engine of the Girvan–Newman community algorithm later — it deletes the highest-betweenness edge.

Eigenvector centrality and PageRank (authority)

Influence where connections to high-scoring nodes count more than connections to low-scoring ones. Google's PageRank is a variant of eigenvector centrality for directed graphs. Mechanically: iterate a stochastic transition matrix \( M \) against a score vector until convergence (power iteration).

Power iteration (slide 28)\[ \begin{bmatrix}\tfrac13\\[2pt]\tfrac13\\[2pt]\tfrac13\end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix}\tfrac13\\[2pt]\tfrac12\\[2pt]\tfrac16\end{bmatrix} \to \begin{bmatrix}\tfrac{5}{12}\\[2pt]\tfrac13\\[2pt]\tfrac14\end{bmatrix} \to \cdots \to \begin{bmatrix}\tfrac25\\[2pt]\tfrac25\\[2pt]\tfrac15\end{bmatrix} \]
Drill the why · why "random walkers" instead of plain matrix convergence?

Real graphs have loops, disconnected components, and dangling links (nodes with no outgoing edge). Plain matrix iteration can get stuck or fail to converge on these. Modeling it as random walkers — with a small teleport probability to a random node — guarantees the walk can always escape, handles scale, and is what makes PageRank robust. Hold this teleport idea: personalized PageRank later biases exactly that teleport.

eli-intern · the kite network punchline (Krackhardt 1988)

On the classic kite graph, no single node wins all measures. Diane wins degree (0.667) and PageRank (0.147); Fernando and Garth tie for closeness (0.643); but Heather wins betweenness (0.389) because she is the only bridge to the Ike–Jane tail — even though her degree is mediocre. Lesson: pick the centrality that matches your actual question. "Most important" is undefined until you say "important for what."

  2. Because matrix multiplication is impossible on directed graphs.
  3. Because it converts a directed graph into an undirected one.

Community mining — finding clusters

Restate the problem: what makes a set of nodes a "community," and why is finding them generally expensive?

A community is a subset of vertices with dense linkage inside and sparse linkage outside — more tightly connected to each other than to the rest. It's expensive because the search space of possible groupings is combinatorial, and the best methods (betweenness recomputation, clique enumeration, min-cut) involve repeated global calculations over the whole graph.

Defining a community depends on: how links/weights are defined, links to the outside (inter-group heterogeneity), links within (intra-group homogeneity), and whether overlapping communities are allowed. A good layout can already reveal them visually.

Girvan–Newman (divisive)

  1. Calculate the betweenness of all edges.
  2. Remove the edge with the highest betweenness (the bridges between communities).
  3. Recalculate betweenness for affected edges.
  4. Repeat until no edges remain (top-down, like divisive hierarchical clustering). Expensive.
Drill the why · why remove the highest-betweenness edge?

Bridges between communities carry all the shortest paths that cross between groups, so they have the highest edge-betweenness. Cutting them first peels communities apart cleanly. (Exam trap: it removes the highest-betweenness edge, not node.)

Min-cut family

Min cut\[ \sum w_e^c \qquad\text{(sum of cut-edge weights)} \]
Min ratio cut — balances partition sizes\[ \Big(\sum w_e^c\Big)\Big(\tfrac{1}{|A|}+\tfrac{1}{|B|}\Big) \]
Min-max cut — also rewards within-community density\[ \Big(\sum w_e^c\Big)\Big(\tfrac{1}{\sum w_e^A}+\tfrac{1}{\sum w_e^B}\Big) \]

Plain min-cut tends to shave off a single node; the ratio/max variants penalize unbalanced partitions. All expensive.

Louvain (greedy modularity)

  1. Initialize each node as its own community.
  2. Move each node to the neighbouring community that increases modularity the most; repeat until no improvement.
  3. Collapse each community into a supernode; rebuild the graph; repeat from step 2.
Modularity
A quality score for a division into communities: the relative density of edges inside communities vs. edges outside. Higher = better-separated communities.
eli14 · why Louvain is the practical favourite

Girvan–Newman keeps re-computing expensive betweenness; Louvain is greedy and agglomerative — it makes cheap local "should I join my neighbour's club?" decisions, then zooms out by merging clubs into single super-members and repeats. That multi-level collapse is why it scales to huge graphs while Girvan–Newman, min-cut, and clique enumeration choke.

Cliques, k-cores, and bottom-up growth

Clique
A subgraph where every node connects to every other. A maximal clique is one not contained in any larger clique. Enumeration is very expensive; perfect cliques are rare.
Near-clique
A relaxation — not every pair must connect.
k-core
A maximal subset of vertices where each is connected to at least \( k \) others in the subset. A practical relaxation of the clique idea.

Bottom-up mining grows a community from a seed node, adding nodes that complete cliques (or k-cores), and can allow overlapping communities. Caveats: most methods assume the entire network is known; detecting overlap is harder; and interpreting why nodes cluster is non-trivial.

Which best contrasts Girvan–Newman with Louvain?

  1. Girvan–Newman is supervised; Louvain is unsupervised.

Featurization and network-based inference

Restate the problem: most nodes are unlabeled; a few are known fraudsters. How can the structure of the graph help you label the rest, and what assumption makes this even possible?

If "birds of a feather flock together" (homophily), then a node connected to known fraudsters is itself more likely fraudulent — "guilt by association." This turns the problem into semi-supervised learning on a graph. The simplifying assumption is the Markov property: a node's outcome depends only on its first-order (1-hop) neighbours, which makes the math tractable. It's an assumption, but a valid one in many settings.

Two roads: network learning vs. featurization

  • Network learning — use the network structure directly to infer labels (propagation/collective inference).
  • Featurization — extract numeric features from the network, then feed any classifier.
  • Often both: the predictions of a network learner become features.

Homophily — the foundation

Assess it by looking at edge distribution vs. node class. With homophily: same-class edges are more common than between-class. Without: edges are class-blind (random). Evidence: fraudsters cluster (shared resources, identity theft); a customer strongly tied to a recently-churned customer is more likely to churn; Fixman et al. (2016) found people call others of similar income.

Relational Neighbor Classifier

The simplest relational learner: class probability = fraction of neighbours with that class.

Neighbours = {NF, F, F, NF, NF}, Z = 5\[ P(F\mid ?) = \tfrac{2}{5}, \qquad P(NF\mid ?) = \tfrac{3}{5} \]

Uses the Markov property (1-hop only). Simple — "but boring."

Propagation / collective inference

Models diffusion through the network (behavior cascading node-to-node like an epidemic — Kleinberg 2007): rumors, public health, financial contagion, viral marketing. Methods listed: Gibbs sampling, iterative classification, relaxation labeling, loopy belief propagation, personalized PageRank, label propagation. These go beyond 1-hop by propagating iteratively.

Personalized PageRank

In normal PageRank the teleport jumps to a node chosen uniformly. In personalized PageRank, the teleport distribution is biased toward a class of interest (\( Y=1 \)) via the teleport probability \( \alpha \). "Lazy random walks" may rest at a vertex. The propagated scores become predictions.

Drill the why · why does personalized PageRank "leak" more than plain centrality?

Because its teleport is biased using the label information (it jumps toward known positives). So a personalized-PageRank feature secretly encodes the labels. Plain degree/closeness/betweenness use only structure, no labels — they leak far less. Remember this for the validation section: label-using features are leakage-prone.

Label propagation / spreading

Iterative semi-supervised label diffusion (Zhu and Ghahramani 2002 = propagation; Zhou et al. 2004 = label spreading). Huang et al. (2020) provocatively showed "combining label propagation and simple models out-performs GNNs." Convergence is not always straightforward, especially in one-class or regression settings.

Relational logistic regression (Lu and Getoor 2003)

Combine local attributes (age, income, RFM) with network attributes from neighbours in one logistic model. Three ways to summarize neighbour classes:

mode-link
most frequent neighbour class.
count-link
frequency of each neighbour class.
binary-link
binary indicator of each class's presence.

Worked row, neighbours {NF, F, F, NF, NF}: mode-link = NF; freq-NF = 3, freq-F = 2; binary-NF = 1, binary-F = 1. You can add centrality metrics, community labels, relational-learner probabilities — any feature, any classifier.

Featurization summary (the recommended recipe)

Keep the network simple; nodes carry labels and attributes; non-directional edges (with extra relationships); domain-driven features on egonets (degree/triangle/path/closeness/betweenness/PageRank split by class); and personalized PageRank as a "global network" feature. An egonet is the subgraph around a focal node.

The "Markov property" as used for network inference here means…

  1. All edge weights leaving a node must sum to one.
  2. A node's outcome is determined only by its first-order (1-hop) neighbours.
  3. The graph must be acyclic for inference to converge.

How does personalized PageRank differ from plain PageRank?

  1. It removes the teleport step entirely so the walk never restarts.
  2. It only works on undirected graphs.
  3. Its teleport distribution is biased toward a class of interest instead of uniform.

A word on validation — the leakage trap

Restate the problem: in normal ML you split train/test, then engineer features on train only and re-apply to test. Why does this rule break on a graph?

Because graph features are extracted from the whole graph's structure. A test node's neighbours may sit in the training set (and vice versa), so when you compute its degree, triangles, or PageRank you are implicitly using information that crosses the split — data leakage. Plus labels and structure both change over time. A naive split doesn't just risk leakage; it breaks feature generation because neighbourhoods straddle the boundary.

Transductive vs. inductive evaluation

Transductive

Test on the same graph the model saw, with some nodes' labels masked during training. Easy (same graph at train and inference). But masked nodes still message-pass their features to scored nodes → leakage. Widely used in papers anyway.

Inductive

Test on a separate graph not seen during training. Requires GNN ops independent of node/edge count. But splitting one graph into two means cutting edges removes information. A temporal split helps, yet overlapping time windows still leak.

No strategy is perfect

There is large overlap between train and test structure either way. Mitigations: make the time gap large and test at multiple points; randomly censor positive labels during feature generation; prefer features that leak less — personalized PageRank uses labels (leaks), other centrality metrics do not; neighbour-only domain features (no labels) are safer. At prediction time you also need the up-to-date network state → stricter data requirements.

Broader context

This is an active research critique, not settled doctrine: Shchur et al. (2018) — "existing GNN evaluation strategies have serious shortcomings"; and notably Guo & vanden Broucke (2024), "A Critique on Transductive Evaluation for GNN Node Classification." (Your course staff are authors — exam-likely.)

Why does transductive evaluation still leak even when you mask the test nodes' labels?

  1. Because masking accidentally also reveals the labels to the loss function.
  2. Because a high learning rate memorizes the masked nodes.
  3. Masked nodes still message-pass their features into the nodes whose loss is computed.

Shallow embeddings: DeepWalk and node2vec

Restate the problem: you want a fixed-length vector for each node (so any classifier can use it) that preserves graph structure. word2vec already turns words into vectors from sentences. How would you reuse it on a graph — what plays the role of "sentences"?

Random walks are the "sentences" and nodes are the "words." Walk randomly from each node to produce sequences; nodes that co-occur in walks are "context" for each other; feed these to skip-gram (word2vec). The learned embeddings place structurally-similar nodes near each other in vector space. That is exactly DeepWalk.

Random walk (the building block)

Start at a vertex; each step move to another vertex. Unweighted → next vertex chosen uniformly among neighbours. Weighted → probability proportional to edge weight. (Same idea as PageRank.)

DeepWalk (Perozzi et al. 2014)

An unsupervised method learning low-dimensional node embeddings that preserve structure. Generate random walks → train skip-gram on them. Embeddings feed node classification, link prediction, clustering.

DeepWalk(G, w, d, gamma, t):       # window w, embed dim d, gamma walks/vertex, length t
  Phi ~ Uniform(|V| x d)           # init embedding matrix
  build binary tree T from V       # for hierarchical softmax
  for i in 0..gamma:
    shuffle(V)
    for v_i in V:
      W = RandomWalk(G, v_i, t)     # one short walk
      SkipGram(Phi, W, w)          # update Phi toward predicting walk-neighbours
SkipGram objective per (center node, context node)\[ J(\Phi) = -\log \Pr\!\big(u_k \mid \Phi(v_j)\big), \qquad \Phi \leftarrow \Phi - \alpha\,\frac{\partial J}{\partial \Phi} \]

node2vec (Grover and Leskovec 2016)

Same recipe, one innovation: a biased 2nd-order random walk. The search bias depends on the distance \( d_{tx} \) from the previous node \( t \):

node2vec search bias\[ \alpha_{pq}(t,x) = \begin{cases} \tfrac{1}{p} & d_{tx}=0 \ \ (\text{return to } t)\\[4pt] 1 & d_{tx}=1\\[4pt] \tfrac{1}{q} & d_{tx}=2 \end{cases} \]

The return parameter \( p \) discourages/encourages backtracking; the in-out parameter \( q \) trades BFS-like exploration (stay local → captures structural roles) against DFS-like exploration (wander far → captures communities). Steering the walk steers the "vocabulary," hence the representation.

Three fatal drawbacks of shallow embeddings
  • Computationally inefficient: no parameters shared between nodes — the encoder is basically a lookup table, so parameters grow linearly with the number of nodes.
  • Limited representation: hard to include node/edge features.
  • No generalization: cannot handle dynamic graphs — no inference on new nodes (transductive only).
eli-intern · "no parameter sharing," concretely

DeepWalk literally stores a separate learned vector per node, with nothing tying them together. Add a new node tomorrow and there's no function to produce its embedding — you'd have to retrain. GNNs fix this by learning a shared transformation (the same weights applied at every node), so they generalize to unseen nodes. That single difference — shared weights — is the whole motivation for moving from shallow embeddings to GNNs.

8

What is node2vec's key innovation over DeepWalk?

  1. It replaces skip-gram with a convolutional neural network.
  2. A biased 2nd-order walk (parameters p, q) steering between BFS-like and DFS-like exploration.
  3. It makes the embeddings inductive so new nodes need no retraining.

Graph Neural Networks — feeding a graph to a net

Restate the problem: we want one architecture that learns representations for nodes/edges/whole-graphs, uses both features and structure, and generalizes to unseen elements. The obvious move is to feed the adjacency matrix into a DNN. Why is that a bad idea?

The adjacency matrix is (1) very sparse and (2) order-dependent: relabeling the nodes permutes its rows/columns, and a plain DNN is not permutation invariant — many adjacency matrices encode the same graph yet would give different outputs. So you'd be training the net to depend on an arbitrary node ordering. Adjacency lists (store edge \( e_k \) as a tuple \( (i,j) \)) are memory-efficient and avoid this.

The four kinds of information and embedding features

A graph carries up to four things: node features, edge features, global features, and connectivity. Features go in as a feature matrix easily; connectivity is the hard part. To embed features, don't use a categorical embedding layer (we're not mapping an integer to a vector) — use a standard MLP:

Per-element MLP embedding, stacked over k layers\[ x_i^{1} = \mathrm{MLP}\!\big(x_i^{0}\big), \qquad x_i^{k} = \mathrm{MLP}\!\big(x_i^{k-1}\big) \]

Problem 1 — task/object mismatch → pooling

You may have info on edges but want predictions on nodes (or the whole graph). Pooling fixes the mismatch: for each item to predict, gather the relevant embeddings, concatenate, then aggregate (sum or mean). A graph-level prediction's pooling is just like global average pooling at the end of a CNN → one output vector.

Problem 2 — not using connectivity → message passing

A plain GNN that is just an MLP over node features never uses connectivity during learning — each node is processed independently, and connectivity only enters at pooling time. We want embeddings to be connectivity-aware before pooling. Enter message passing: for each node, gather neighbour embeddings (the messages), aggregate them (sum/mean), and pass the result through an update function (a learned net).

Generic message passing\[ x_i^{k} = \gamma^{k}\!\Big(\ x_i^{k-1},\ \ \mathrm{AGG}_{\,j\in N(i)}\ \varphi^{k}\big(x_i^{k-1},\,x_j^{k-1},\,e_{j,i}\big)\ \Big) \]
  • \( \varphi \) — the gather / message function (what one neighbour sends).
  • \( \mathrm{AGG} \) — the aggregator (permutation-invariant: sum/mean/max).
  • \( \gamma \) — the update function (e.g. an MLP).
Simplified form — "sum of products over my neighbours"\[ x_i^{k} = \gamma^{k}\!\Big(\ \sum_{j\in N(i)\cup\{i\}} x_j^{k-1}\, W_{j,i}\ \Big) \]
Drill the why · message passing IS convolution on irregular domains

An image is a regular grid graph: each pixel is a node, its neighbours are the adjacent pixels, and the grid's adjacency matrix is banded. A CNN convolution sums a weighted combination of a pixel's neighbours — which is exactly "aggregate neighbour values with weights." So a CNN is message passing on a regular graph, and a GNN generalizes it to irregular graphs where each node has a different number of neighbours.

The robustness caveat

The simplified form's weakness is \( W_{j,i} \): a separate weight per node-pair doesn't share parameters and won't generalize. The fix is a gather function \( \varphi \) with weight sharing — the same learnable transform applied to every neighbour. (Same lesson as DeepWalk's drawback: shared weights are what give generalization.)

Spatial generic form (the symbol is a permutation-invariant aggregation)\[ x'_i = \gamma_\Theta\!\Big( x_i,\ \bigoplus_{j\in N(i)} \varphi_\Theta\big(x_i, x_j, e_{j,i}\big)\Big) \]
"Localized" is not really a drawback

"Neighbourhood" can be defined loosely (1-hop + 2-hop, or a sample). And stacking GNN layers widens the receptive field — just like depth in a CNN — so a deep stack builds globally-aware embeddings step by step. Message passing is the generic approach (propagate → aggregate → update); spectral methods are a special case that does not aggregate over neighbours and usually avoids stacking many layers.

GCN (Kipf and Welling 2016)

"Semi-supervised classification with graph convolutional networks." Per node:

GCN per-node\[ x'_i = \Theta^{\top} \sum_{j\in N(i)\cup\{i\}} \frac{e_{j,i}}{\sqrt{\hat d_j\,\hat d_i}}\ x_j, \qquad \hat d_i = 1 + \sum_{j\in N(i)} e_{j,i} \]
GCN one-shot matrix form\[ X' = \hat D^{-1/2}\,\hat A\,\hat D^{-1/2}\,X\,\Theta, \qquad \hat A = A + I,\ \ \hat D_{ii}=\textstyle\sum_j \hat A_{ij} \]

\( \hat A = A + I \) adds self-loops (a node includes its own features); \( \hat D^{-1/2}\cdots\hat D^{-1/2} \) is symmetric normalization so high-degree neighbours don't dominate; \( \Theta \) is the learnable kernel. (Reflection: are those weights part of the update or the message function?)

GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al. 2017) — inductive

A general inductive framework: embed a node by sampling and aggregating its local neighbourhood.

h_v^0 = x_v
for k = 1..K:
  for each node v:
    h_N(v)^k = AGGREGATE_k({ h_u^{k-1} : u in N(v) })   # N applies SAMPLING
    h_v^k    = sigma( W^k . CONCAT(h_v^{k-1}, h_N(v)^k) )
    h_v^k    = h_v^k / ||h_v^k||_2                        # L2 normalize
z_v = h_v^K
Simplified mean-aggregator form\[ x'_i = W_1 x_i + W_2 \cdot \operatorname*{mean}_{j\in N(i)} x_j \]
Drill the why · what makes GraphSAGE inductive?

It learns aggregator weights \( W^k \), not a per-node lookup table. The same learned function "look at my sampled neighbours, aggregate, concat with myself, transform" applies to any node — including ones never seen at training time. So a brand-new node gets an embedding without retraining. Neighbour sampling also keeps cost bounded on huge graphs (in PyTorch Geometric the GNN unit and the sampler — NeighborLoader — are separate).

GAT (Veličković et al. 2017) — attention

Folds self-attention into the propagation step: a node's new state is a weighted sum over neighbours, where attention coefficients \( \alpha_{ij} \) are a softmax over a LeakyReLU-activated score from a weight vector \( a\in\mathbb{R}^{2F'} \). Supports multi-head attention (e.g. \( K=3 \)) with heads concatenated or averaged.

eli5 · GCN vs. GraphSAGE vs. GAT in one breath

All three "ask the neighbours and combine." GCN: ask all neighbours, weight each by a fixed degree-based rule. GraphSAGE: ask a random sample of neighbours (so new nodes work and big graphs stay cheap), then concat with yourself. GAT: ask all neighbours but learn how much to listen to each (attention). Same skeleton — message, aggregate, update — different aggregation rule.

General pipeline and learning tasks

A GNN block = optional Sampling Operator → Conv/Recurrent Operator → optional Pooling Operator, plus optional skip connections; output = node/edge/graph embedding; training = supervised / semi / unsupervised. Tasks:

  • Node-level: classification/regression/clustering (e.g. detect fake accounts).
  • Edge-level: predict an edge's label or whether an edge exists = link prediction (e.g. will a user like a product).
  • Graph-level: classify/regress whole graphs (e.g. molecule toxicity from structure).
11

Why is GraphSAGE inductive whereas DeepWalk is not?

  1. GraphSAGE ignores neighbours entirely, so node count never matters.
  2. GraphSAGE learns a shared aggregator function, so an unseen node's embedding comes from sampling its neighbours — no retraining.
  3. DeepWalk uses self-loops while GraphSAGE does not.
12

In the GCN matrix form \( X' = \hat D^{-1/2}\hat A\,\hat D^{-1/2} X\Theta \), what does \( \hat A = A + I \) accomplish?

  1. It removes all existing edges and keeps only self-connections.
  2. It normalizes the degrees so hubs do not dominate.
  3. It adds self-loops so each node aggregates its own features along with neighbours'.

Graph databases: NoSQL → NewSQL → graph DBs

Restate the problem: a relational DB needs a separate "cross table" plus a multi-way JOIN to answer "which books did author X write?" in a many-to-many setting. What is a graph database's pitch for being better here — and what is the surprising twist about its relationship to relations?

A graph DB stores relationships directly and semantically (an Author WROTE a Book), so you navigate (traverse) them instead of reconstructing them with JOINs at query time. The twist: although it's a NoSQL family, graph DBs go the opposite way from other NoSQL — they increase relational modeling rather than dropping relations. A graph DB is like a "hyper-relational" database where JOIN tables are replaced by first-class, navigable relationships.

Big-data backdrop

Hadoop ⇄ MapReduce ⇄ NoSQL: the Hadoop world (great at volume, bad at querying) tried to add query power, while the database world (great at querying, bad at scaling) tried to scale out. A graph database (Neo4j, Memgraph) is a NoSQL database.

RDBMS vs. NoSQL

RDBMS: tables with primary keys, linked by foreign keys (one-to-many; many-to-many needs a cross table), queried with SQL, schema-driven, strong consistency, ACID. NoSQL: driven by Big Data (Volume, Variety, Velocity), flexible/no fixed schema, massive horizontal scalability, often favours availability/performance over consistency, BASE with eventual consistency.

ACID (relational)

Atomicity · Consistency · Isolation · Durability. The whole DB is consistent at all times.

BASE (NoSQL)

Basically Available · Soft state · Eventual consistency. Replicas converge eventually after a transaction, not continuously.

Exam traps
  • "NoSQL" was first used in 1998 for a relational DB that simply forewent SQL — unrelated to the modern movement. Read it as "not only SQL" / "not relational."
  • Eventual consistency = replicas become consistent at some point, not continuously guaranteed.
  • Graph DBs increase relational modeling — the common false statement is "all NoSQL drops relations."
  • Neo4j is optimized for OLTP and was not originally for graph analytics; the Neo4j GDS library (and Memgraph MAGE) added that later.

NewSQL and new data paradigms

The line blurred: relational vendors adopted horizontal scalability, dropped strict schema, allowed nested/JSON in tables — but kept a strong SQL backend (e.g. CockroachDB). The most interesting databases bring new paradigms, not just distributed storage: time-series (InfluxDB, TimescaleDB, Kdb+), geospatial (PostgreSQL+PostGIS), and graph databases. Implementations differ: Neo4j stores nodes/edges with type + attributes + unique id; FlockDB (Twitter) stores only a simplified directed edge list (source/destination + state + sort position) — built for speed/scale over expressiveness.

Cypher (Neo4j's query language)

Declarative, text-based, SQL-like, but built around graph patterns via a special MATCH clause. Syntax: nodes are parentheses (), type-filtered with a colon (b:Book); edges are -- (undirected) or --> (directed); relationship type goes in square brackets -[:WRITTEN_BY]-.

SQL (three tables + join)

SELECT title
FROM books, authors, books_authors
WHERE author.id = books_authors.author_id
  AND books.id  = books_authors.book_id
  AND author.name = "Seppe vanden Broucke"

Cypher (nodes + relationships)

MATCH (b:Book)<-[:WRITTEN_BY]-(a:Author)
WHERE a.name = "Seppe vanden Broucke"
RETURN b.title

JOINs become pattern matching. Distinct customers over 30 who paid cash for a Seppe book:

MATCH (c:Customer)-[p:PURCHASED]->(b:Book)<-[:WRITTEN_BY]-(a:Author)
WHERE a.name = "Seppe" AND c.age > 30 AND p.type = "cash"
RETURN DISTINCT c.name;

Variable-length / transitive paths use * with optional min..max — trivial in Cypher, hard in SQL even with recursive extensions. All books in "Programming" and any subcategory:

MATCH (b:Book)-[:IN_GENRE]->(:Genre)-[:PARENT*0..]-(tg:Genre)
WHERE tg.name = "Programming"
RETURN b.title;
Drill the why · why is the recursive query "hard in SQL"?

SQL's table-join model has no native notion of "follow this relationship an arbitrary number of times." You'd need recursive CTEs that are clunky and slow. A graph traversal — "walk PARENT edges from this genre as far as they go" — is exactly what graph DBs are built for, so -[:PARENT*0..]- expresses it in one clause.
