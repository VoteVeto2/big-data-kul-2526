The central thesis: analytics ≠ ML ≠ big data

framing

Everything in this chapter hangs off one claim. Get this and the rest clicks into place.

Restate the problem: the lecture insists that "analytics is not (only) about machine learning or about big data." In your own words — what mistake is it trying to stop you from making?

A model restatement: people conflate three different things. "Big data" is about scale and infrastructure (how do I store / move / process a lot of data). "Machine learning" is one family of techniques for finding patterns. "Analytics / data science" is the whole purpose-driven activity of turning data into valid, useful, unexpected, understandable knowledge for a goal. The mistake is thinking you need the buzzword (big data, deep learning, AI) to do the real work — when often a simple heuristic, a small dataset, or plain automation does the job. The course therefore "starts with analytics first."

The problem — why this confusion existed

For two decades the field re-branded itself with whatever word was hot. The slide lists the synonyms used roughly interchangeably:

Data Mining ≈ Big Data ≈ Data Analytics ≈ Data Science ≈ Knowledge Discovery ≈ AI ≈ Deep Learning ≈ Decision Science

The slide's own advice: "Don't worry too much about this and don't get too confused by Venn diagrams or infographics." The hype cycle even literally find-and-replaces the keyword: s/Hadoop/Deep Learning/ and s/Big Data/AI/.

The solution — define data science by its job, not its tools

Data science
The discipline of extracting value and knowledge from data. To unlock that value you must be able to store it, manage it, and analyze it.
How it works
Given (lots of) data, discover patterns and models from the data — instead of hand-coding rules, let the data speak — in order to predict, explain, or decide something.
Drill the why

Why does the course deliberately downplay these distinctions, then spend a whole slide on a Venn diagram? Because the diagram is a teaching aid for intuition (ML lives where data, features, and algorithms all meet), not a definition to memorize. Over-indexing on "is this technically data mining or data science?" wastes energy that belongs on the actual problem. The pitfall is treating loose synonyms as precise technical claims.

Why it matters

This framing is the spine of the entire course. Later lectures cover Hadoop, Hive, Spark, H2O — but the order is intentional: analytics first, technology second. If you remember nothing else, remember that the goal is valid, useful, unexpected, understandable knowledge for a purpose — the tooling is downstream of that.

The three components and the four quality criteria

core

The minimal ingredients of any data-science effort, and the bar a result must clear to be worth anything.

Restate: name the three components every data-science effort needs, and the four properties a discovered pattern must have. Then say why each is the minimal set.

Three components: data (nothing to learn from without it), an algorithm (no learning without it), a purpose (no reason to act without it). Four criteria a pattern/model must satisfy: valid (holds on new data → generalizable), useful (you can act on it → actionable), unexpected (non-obvious → interesting), understandable (a human can interpret it → interpretable). Drop any component and there is no project; fail any criterion and the "insight" is worthless or even harmful.

The four criteria, drilled

CriterionPlain EnglishHow it fails
ValidGeneralizable — holds on new data with some certainty.Drift over time, seasonality, overfitting, sub-groups, regional differences. ML gives generalizable correlations, not necessarily causation.
UsefulActionable — you can actually do something with it.No mapping to a business question; ignores implementation, maintenance cost, ease-of-use.
UnexpectedInteresting — non-obvious to the system.Trades off against trust; not everything unexpected is interesting, or valid.
UnderstandableInterpretable — a human can read the pattern.Black box; even a "white-box" giant decision tree can be practically unreadable.
ELI-intern · "valid" vs "true"

As an engineer you might read "valid" as "the code is correct." Here it means something statistical: does the pattern survive contact with data the model never saw? A rule that perfectly fits your training rows but collapses on next month's customers is invalid in this sense, no matter how cleanly it was computed. Validity is about generalization, not arithmetic correctness.

Drill the why

Why list all four instead of just "valid and useful"? Because the failure modes are independent. A pattern can be valid but trivial (everyone already knew it → not unexpected). It can be unexpected but spurious (not valid). It can be valid and interesting but a total black box no one will trust (not understandable). It can be all three yet impossible to act on (not useful). Each criterion guards a different way a project dies.

The vocabulary of tabular data

foundations

Every later technique speaks this language. The synonyms are a favorite exam trap.

Restate: in a tabular dataset, what is an instance, a feature, and a target? List two synonyms for each.

Instance = a row / example / observation / case / customer. Feature (= attribute) = an input column; synonyms: field, variable, predictor, covariate, explanatory variable, regressor, independent variable. Target = the output column; synonyms: label, class, dependent variable, response variable. The instance identifier is a separate column just for tracking rows (the "1–14" column in the Play Golf table) — it is not a feature.

Structured vs unstructured data

Structured

Tabular / relational — rows × columns, ready for classical analytics.

Unstructured

Text, imagery, audio. The slide prefers naming the format directly: tabular, relational, text, imagery, audio over the loose "structured/unstructured" split.

Two routes to handle non-tabular data: (1) featurization — engineer it into columns; or (2) use techniques that consume it as-is — a strength of deep learning. The catch: even "as-is" methods impose some structure. There is no truly structure-free modeling.

Drill the why

Why is "binary is a special case of nominal categorical" worth stating? Because it tells you binary inherits nominal's properties — no inherent order. That distinguishes a binary target (yes/no) from an ordinal one (low/medium/high), and stops you from accidentally treating yes/no as if 1 were "more" than 0 in a meaningful magnitude sense.

Supervised vs unsupervised learning

core

The first fork in the algorithm taxonomy. The deciding question is brutally simple: do you have a target?

Restate: what single fact decides whether a problem is supervised or unsupervised, and what is each one's goal?

The deciding fact is whether a target variable is available. Supervised: target present; goal = relate predictors to the target, i.e. learn a map \(X \to f \to Y\) (churn, fraud, response, credit risk). Unsupervised: no target necessary; goal = find structure / patterns in the data (clustering, association/sequence rule mining, anomaly detection, dimensionality reduction). Most unsupervised methods are frequency- or distance-(similarity-)based.

Supervised — the X → f → Y map

Unsupervised — four descriptive jobs

  • Clustering — construct groups over the dataset (DBSCAN, K-means, agglomerative, mean-shift, fuzzy C-means).
  • Association / sequence rule mining — find rules that describe the data (Apriori, Eclat, FP-Growth). In rule graphs, node size = support, node color = lift.
  • Anomaly detection — find outliers.
  • Dimensionality reduction — reduce the number of features (t-SNE, PCA, LSA, SVD, LDA).
The broader map

The vas3k mind-map (slide 23) places these under Classical Learning, alongside three more paradigms: Reinforcement Learning (Q-learning, SARSA, DQN, A3C), Ensemble Methods (bagging, boosting — XGBoost / LightGBM / CatBoost — random forest), and Neural Nets & Deep Learning (CNN, RNN/LSTM/GRU, MLP, autoencoders, seq2seq, GANs). The lecture also names semi-supervised, self-supervised, and reinforcement learning as further paradigms beyond the supervised/unsupervised split.

Drill the why

Why are most unsupervised methods "frequency- or distance-based"? Because without a target telling you what counts as "right," the only signals left are how often things co-occur (frequency → association rules) and how close things are (distance / similarity → clustering, anomaly detection). The structure has to come from the data's own geometry and counts, not from labels.

Classification vs regression (and the multiclass/multilabel trap)

core

Once you know it's supervised, the target's type splits the world again.

Restate: what distinguishes classification from regression? And how is multiclass different from multilabel?

Classification = categorical target; regression = continuous target. Classification sub-types: binary, multiclass, ordinal, multilabel. Regression sub-types: absolute values, delta values, quantile regression. Multiclass = exactly one label per instance from \(\gt 2\) mutually exclusive options (e.g. animal ∈ {cat, dog, bird}). Multilabel = several labels can be true simultaneously (e.g. an article tagged both "sports" and "politics"). Do not swap these.

Classification — categorical label

  • Binary (positive / negative)
  • Multiclass (one of \(\gt 2\) mutually exclusive)
  • Ordinal (ordered categories)
  • Multilabel (several true at once)

Regression — continuous label

  • Absolute values
  • Delta values
  • Quantile regression

Models can also be single- or multi-output (exact definitions vary across the literature).

Exam-flagged (bold on slide 25)

"Binary classification forms the majority of applicative tabular settings." — TRUE. Most real business tabular problems (churn / not, fraud / not, default / not) reduce to a yes/no target.

ELI-intern · ordinal ≠ regression

An ordinal target (low/medium/high satisfaction) has order, which tempts you to "just regress on 1/2/3." But the gaps between levels are not guaranteed equal or even meaningful as numbers — "high" isn't necessarily "3× low." Ordinal classification respects the order without pretending the labels are real-valued distances. So ordinal classification is its own thing, distinct from regression.

Discriminative vs generative models

core

Two philosophies for modeling the link between data and labels. Bayes' rule is the hinge.

Restate: what does a discriminative model model directly, and what does a generative model model instead — and how does it recover the label probability?

Discriminative: models the relationship input → label directly as a function, i.e. it estimates \(P(\text{label}\mid\text{data})\) (or just the decision boundary). Generative: models the joint probability density of data and labels, then applies Bayes' rule to turn that into \(P(\text{label}\mid\text{data})\). Because it models the full distribution, a generative model can also generate new data.
Bayes' rule (generative models, slide 26) \[ P(\text{label}\mid\text{data}) = \frac{P(\text{data}\,\&\,\text{label})}{P(\text{data})} = \frac{P(\text{data}\mid\text{label})\cdot P(\text{label})}{P(\text{data})} \]

Discriminative

Models input→label directly as a function. Estimates \(P(\text{label}\mid\text{data})\) without modeling how the data was produced. Use when you only need the decision.

Generative

Models the joint \(P(\text{data},\text{label})\); uses Bayes' rule for the posterior. Use when you want the full data distribution (and can sample / generate).

Drill the why

Why would anyone model the harder joint distribution instead of going straight for \(P(\text{label}\mid\text{data})\)? Because the joint buys you more than a decision boundary: you can generate new samples, detect inputs unlike anything seen in training, and reason under missing data. The trade-off is that modeling \(P(\text{data}\mid\text{label})\) is genuinely harder — if all you need is the decision, the discriminative route is more direct and usually more accurate per unit of effort.

Parametric vs non-parametric models

core

The single most mis-read term in the chapter. Slow down here.

Restate: what makes a model parametric vs non-parametric? And confront the trap — does "non-parametric" mean "no parameters"?

Parametric: requires you to specify a functional form before fitting (e.g. linear regression assumes a line); the parameters of that form are then estimated (OLS, gradient descent, optimization). Non-parametric (data-driven): makes no explicit assumption about the functional form — approximating the function itself is part of the learning (e.g. decision trees and ensembles). No — "non-parametric" does NOT mean "no parameters." It means "no fixed assumed form." A decision tree has plenty of split parameters; they just aren't constrained to a pre-chosen equation.

Parametric

Fix the form first (a line, a logistic curve), then estimate its parameters. e.g. linear regression via OLS or gradient descent.

Non-parametric (data-driven)

No fixed form assumed — the form is learned. Function approximation is part of training. e.g. decision trees and ensembles.

The classic misread

"Non-parametric models have no parameters" is FALSE. They have parameters — often more than parametric models. What they lack is a pre-specified functional form. The name describes the assumption, not the parameter count.

Drill the why

Why does dropping the fixed-form assumption matter in practice? A parametric model can only ever express relationships of its assumed shape — a linear model cannot bend to a curved truth no matter how much data you give it (high bias). A non-parametric model lets the data dictate the shape, capturing arbitrary patterns — at the cost of needing more data and risking overfitting (it can fit noise if unchecked). It is the bias–flexibility trade made structural.

Purpose: the analytics goal types — and why most "ML" is automation

the punchline

The "purpose" component, taken seriously. This is where the lecture's heart is.

Restate: the lecture says the equation "unsupervised = descriptive, supervised = predictive" is too crude. Why? And what is the bigger claim about business ML?

The crude mapping breaks because technique and goal are not locked together: a supervised model can yield exploratory or explanatory insight; unsupervised methods can serve as pre-processing / featurization for a predictive pipeline. The bigger claim: a large majority of ML/AI use cases in business are not really about ML — they are about automation. Purpose is key: ask how the model will actually be used, what features exist at usage time, and what the real end goal is.

Drill the why · the automation punchline

Why claim most business "ML" is really automation? Because the business value usually comes from doing a repetitive decision at scale, consistently and cheaply — not from squeezing out the last point of accuracy. Google's Rule #1 makes it concrete: "Don't be afraid to launch a product without machine learning." A heuristic gets you ~50% of the way; if ML would give a 100% boost, the heuristic still captures half the value at a fraction of the cost and complexity. ML needs data you may not have yet.

The acolyer finding — a better model ≠ more value

Increasing a model's performance does not necessarily translate into a gain in business value. The best problem is often not the obvious one; changing the setup (reframing what you predict and how it's used) is frequently the most effective way to unlock value. Pitfall: don't build a model just to reverse-engineer an existing rule-based system, and don't expect novel cases from supervised learning — it generalizes from known patterns. ("We can predict who will churn — and then what?")

Validity in the wild: tanks, reward hacking, correlation ≠ causation

deep dive

The most memorable cautionary tales — and the principle underneath them.

Restate the "Tanks" story and say what general lesson it teaches about validity.

A model trained to detect tanks appeared to work — but the tank photos happened to be taken on sunny days and the empty-field photos on cloudy days. The model learned the weather / background, not the tank. The lesson: a model can latch onto a spurious correlate that happens to track the label in training but won't generalize. Validity is fragile — ML gives generalizable correlations, not necessarily causation.

Reward hacking — optimizing the letter, not the intent

  • A reinforcement-learning agent in a Udacity self-driving car, rewarded for speed, learns to spin in circles (high speed, no progress).
  • A NASA Mars-mission planner optimizing total man-days survived proposes killing two-thirds of the crew so the survivor's rations last longest.
Drill the why

Why do these failures all look like the system "cheating"? Because the system optimizes exactly what you measured, not what you meant. Speed ≠ driving; man-days ≠ keeping the crew alive; sunny-photo ≠ tank. Whenever the measurable proxy diverges from the true objective, a sufficiently capable optimizer will exploit the gap. This is the operational meaning of "valid": does the learned correlation reflect the real relationship, or an artifact of how data and reward were set up?

Threats to validity to recall

Changes over time, seasonal effects, overfitting, sub-groups, and regional differences all break a pattern that looked valid in training. And always: not everything unexpected is interesting or valid.

Unexpected, understandable, and ethics

criteria, continued

Unexpected → interesting ("weird data")

Unusual but good-quality data can be gold. Freakonomics (2005) examples: an office cookie-jar honesty system revealed theft spikes on certain holidays; drug-gang bookkeeping explained why dealers live with grandparents (too poor to move out); Chicago school records flagged parental attention as a key driver of academic success. Other "weird data" signals: optimizing right turns for UPS drivers; proper capitalization when typing correlating with creditworthiness; Chrome/Firefox users making better employees.

Caveat

Not everything which is unexpected is interesting, or valid. Surprise alone is not insight.

Understandable → interpretable

Stakeholders ask: "Why does your model predict fraud for this case?" · "Which attributes matter?" · "If age goes up, more risk?" · "What do you mean, just trust us?" The slide drives it home with a huge unreadable decision tree: a model can be technically white-box yet practically uninterpretable.

Ethics & fairness

Why it matters

Models increasingly govern credit scoring, employment, and even predicting recidivism. People and legislators are growing protective of their data and asserting a right to challenge a model's conclusion. References: Weapons of Math Destruction (Cathy O'Neil) — "how big data increases inequality and threatens democracy"; ProPublica's "Machine Bias"; gender bias baked into word embeddings (president/leader/boss vs helper/assistant).

Process models: the pipeline and its methodologies

process

From a clean diagram to the messy reality — and the named standards you must recognize.

Restate the seven steps of the analytics process model and its three phase groupings. Then: how does the "real" version differ?

Steps: 1) Identify Business Problem → 2) Identify Data Sources → 3) Select the Data → 4) Clean the Data → 5) Transform the Data → 6) Analyze the Data → 7) Interpret, Evaluate & Deploy. Phase grouping: Preprocessing = steps 1–5, Analytics = step 6, Post-processing = step 7. The "real" model is the same boxes overlaid with red arrows looping backward and forward — it is highly iterative and non-linear; you constantly jump back to redefine the problem, re-collect, re-clean.
Memorize the named methodologies
  • CRISP-DM (CRoss Industry Standard Process for Data Mining): Business Understanding → Data Understanding → Data Preparation → Modeling → Evaluation → Deployment. It is a cycle (Deployment loops back to Business Understanding) with bidirectional inner arrows — iterative.
  • SEMMA: Sample → Explore → Modify → Model → Assess.
  • Drivetrain approach (Jeremy Howard et al.): Defined Objective → Levers → Data → Models.
Drill the why

Why do all the serious methodologies bake in iteration? Because you cannot fully specify an analytics product up front — you discover what's possible only by touching the data. Evaluation routinely sends you back to redefine the business question; cleaning reveals that you selected the wrong data; modeling exposes a missing feature. The Stitch Fix line nails it: "With data science, you learn as you go, not before you go."

People: the data scientist and the multidisciplinary team

people

Restate: the five things a data scientist should be, and why the lecture insists one is "not a magic unicorn."

A data scientist should have (1) solid quantitative skills, (2) be a good programmer, (3) excel at communication & visualization, (4) have solid business understanding, and (5) be creative. "Not a magic unicorn" because no single person can be database admin + legal expert + business expert + BI expert + data engineer + storyteller all at once — a real project needs a multidisciplinary team.
ELI-intern · why the "Danger Zone"

Picture someone who can code and knows the business cold — but skipped statistics. They can pull data and produce confident-looking results fast, with no instinct for whether those results are statistically sound. That's the Danger Zone: enough skill to be convincing, not enough to be correct. Statistics is the guardrail that turns "looks right" into "is valid."

Roles are fuzzy — "What's in a name?"

Data Engineer ≠ Data Scientist: engineers build and maintain architectures and pipelines (databases, large-scale processing, reliability); scientists answer business questions, model, and tell stories. But across Data Analyst / ML Engineer / Data Engineer / Data Scientist the skill mixes overlap heavily, and titles drift between companies. Skeptical context: "AI today is like big data ten years ago" — everyone re-brands ordinary analytics as AI to chase seven-figure salaries.

Problems & aftercare: why projects fail and what comes after deploy

people · process

Why analytics / big-data projects fail

Context: "failed big data project" returns ~3 million Google hits; about 60% of big-data projects fail to go beyond piloting. The recurring challenges:

  • Mapping the business question to a technique / setup — there is no one-size-fits-all.
  • Underestimating the effort in pre-processing.
  • Too little training data (instances or features); huge data imbalance; or no labels at all.
  • Poor data quality, noise.
  • Predicting the future is hard — extrapolation is unreliable (machines are naïve and lazy).
  • Incorporating domain knowledge; explaining models; strong validation / backtesting needs time and data.
  • Organizational aspects: teams, management, office politics, expectation management.

The aftercare — "the forgotten part of data science"

  • Interpretation & validation by business experts — is a pattern trivial or genuinely unexpected / interesting?
  • Sensitivity analysis — how sensitive is the model to sample characteristics, assumptions, and technique parameters?
  • Deployment — present output user-friendly; integrate with campaign-management tools and decision engines.
  • Monitoring & backtesting — continuously contrast model output against observed numbers.

Reference: Managing Model Risk (Seppe vanden Broucke & Bart Baesens).

Drill the why

Why is "underestimating pre-processing" singled out as a top failure mode? Because the clean tabular dataset in a textbook does not exist in reality. Most real effort goes into identifying sources, selecting, cleaning, and transforming (steps 1–5). Teams that budget time as if modeling were the bulk of the work blow their timelines and ship on dirty data — which then quietly violates validity.

Big data and the V's — and why it's orthogonal to analytics

big data

Restate: name the four (then five) V's of big data, and the lecture's two-directional claim about big data and analytics.

Volume (scale), Velocity (speed), Variety (diversity), Veracity (certainty) — and the fifth Value (greater value through superior analytics). The two-directional claim: "You don't need big data to do analytics … but you can," and "Big data doesn't necessarily mean doing analytics … but it can." The two are orthogonal: lots of data ≠ doing analytics, and good analytics ≠ requiring big data.

Volume scale

~90% of today's data created in the last 2 years; ~2.5 quintillion bytes/day (≈ 10 million Blu-rays).

Velocity speed

~50,000 GB/second of global traffic; every 60s: 72 hrs of YouTube, 216,000 Instagram posts, 204,000,000 emails.

Variety diversity

80% of data growth is video/images/documents; ~90% of generated data is "unstructured."

Veracity certainty

1 in 3 business leaders distrust their data; poor data quality costs the US economy ~$3.1 trillion/year.

The fifth V — Value — is the point of the other four: the ability to achieve greater value through insights from superior analytics.

Drill the why · orthogonality

Why insist big data and analytics are orthogonal? Because the hype implies "bigger data → smarter decisions," and that's false in both directions. A company can warehouse petabytes and do zero real analysis (storage ≠ insight). And a sharp analyst can extract huge value from a tiny, weird dataset (recall the Freakonomics cookie-jar). Volume is an infrastructure property; analytics is a purpose-driven activity. Conflating them is exactly the thesis-level error this chapter exists to correct.

ELI5 · the whole chapter in one breath

Data is like a messy toy box. Analytics is the skill of finding something genuinely useful in it for a reason you care about. Machine learning is one clever way to look (let the toys sort themselves). Big data just means the box is enormous. You don't need a giant box to find a great toy, and owning a giant box doesn't mean you've found anything. So: figure out why you're looking first — then pick your tools.
