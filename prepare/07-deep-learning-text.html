The big picture: why text needs its own toolkit

motivation

Start at altitude. Before any formula, understand the problem the whole chapter is solving.

Restate the problem in your own words: why can't we just feed a sentence into a logistic regression the way we feed in a table of numbers?
A classifier expects each instance to be a fixed-length vector of numbers. Text is a variable-length sequence of symbols, not numbers — there is no obvious feature vector. It also carries structure that a naive encoding throws away: word order, negation ("not good"), word importance, sarcasm, plus it is dirty (typos, abbreviations, homographs). So the central task is featurization: turn a document into a numeric vector — ideally one where geometric closeness means semantic similarity.

Text is everywhere — medical records, reviews, repair notes, tweets, legislation, emails — but it is unstructured: no straightforward feature-vector representation, written by humans for humans. The chapter mirrors the computer-vision story exactly: a "before vs. after deep learning" split = manual feature engineering vs. automated (learned) feature engineering.

⊛ Drill the why — what is the single unifying idea?

Representation learning. Everything in this chapter — TF-IDF vectors, word2vec, RNN hidden states, attention outputs — is an attempt to find a representation of text such that the downstream task becomes easy. The slides state it bluntly: "the main task of ML and especially deep learning is learning compressed, dense representations that fit the downstream task." The arc of the chapter is just better and better ways to learn that representation.

◇ Broader context — the map of the chapter

Five stages, each fixing the previous one's weakness: (1) classical featurization (BoW, TF-IDF, BM25, n-grams) — sparse, hand-crafted; (2) embeddings (word2vec, GloVe, fastText) — dense, learned, but order-blind; (3) RNNs (LSTM, GRU, seq2seq) — handle order, but forget and are slow; (4) attention & Transformers — look at everything at once; (5) GPT, RLHF, and the post-Transformer frontier. Keep asking at each step: what flaw is this fixing?

Classical featurization: Bag-of-Words, counts, and weighting

stage 1

Corpus terminology and Bag-of-Words

Corpus (plural corpora)
A collection of documents. Usually each document is one instance, but an instance can also be a single sentence.
Token / term
An individual unit of a document — usually a word, but also punctuation, abbreviations, smileys.
Bag-of-Words (BoW)
Treat each document as an unordered collection of tokens. One feature per vocabulary word; value = 1 if present, 0 if absent. Grammar, order, and structure are discarded.

Worked example. Vocabulary = {this, is, an, a, example, second}.

  • "This is an example" → 1 1 1 0 1 0
  • "This is a second example" → 1 1 0 1 1 1
⊛ Drill the why — what does BoW gain and lose?

Gains: the simplest possible numeric representation, cheap to compute, treats every word as a potential keyword. Loses: all word order ("dog bites man" = "man bites dog"), and it causes an explosion of features — one dimension per vocabulary word, mostly zeros. That sparsity is the wound the rest of stage 1 (and embeddings) keeps trying to heal.

Cleaning the vocabulary: normalization, stemming, stop-words

To fight the feature explosion and noise, we shrink the vocabulary:

Normalization
Lowercase everything so "The" and "the" collapse to one feature.
Stemming
Strip suffixes to keep the root (plurals → singular). Porter's algorithm is essentially suffix-stripping. Crude but fast — may produce non-words.
Lemmatization
More principled: map to a real dictionary root, handling irregulars (better → good; flies → fly). May need Part-of-Speech (PoS) tagging when context decides the lemma.
Stop-word removal
Drop very common words ("the", "and", "of", "on") that carry little signal.

From presence to counts: the document-term matrix

BoW with counts instead of binary presence gives the document-term matrix (rows = documents, columns = terms). Raw counts are usually normalized — e.g. divide by total words in the document. Three pitfalls to watch:

  • Unseen terms at inference: a word never seen in training has no column.
  • Document length varies: longer documents have larger raw counts for everything.
  • Frequency extremes: a useful word is not too common, not too rare → impose both an upper and a lower limit on the fraction of documents a word may occur in.

TF-IDF: weighting local importance against global rarity

Raw term frequency makes "the" the most "important" word in every document. What two opposing pressures should a good weighting balance, and which part of TF-IDF supplies each?
Local importance — a term that appears a lot in this document probably matters here (that's TF). Global rarity — a term that appears in every document (like "the") discriminates nothing, so it should be down-weighted (that's IDF, a measure of sparseness). TF-IDF multiplies the two: high TF, high IDF ⇒ a word both frequent here and rare overall ⇒ a strong signal.
Term frequency (term \(t_i\) in document \(d\)) \[ \mathrm{TF}(t_i, d) = \frac{f(t_i, d)}{\lvert d \rvert} \]
Inverse document frequency (over corpus \(c\)) \[ \mathrm{IDF}(t_i) = 1 + \log\!\left( \frac{\lvert c \rvert}{\lvert \{ d \in c : t_i \in d \} \rvert} \right) \]
TF-IDF weight, and the document vector \[ \mathrm{TFIDF}(t_i, d) = \mathrm{TF}(t_i, d)\times \mathrm{IDF}(t_i), \qquad d = [\,w_1, w_2, \dots, w_m\,],\ \ w_i = \mathrm{TFIDF}(t_i, d) \]
⚠ Classic exam trap — read the denominators

The TF denominator is the document length \(\lvert d \rvert\) (how many words are in this document) — not the number of documents. The number of documents \(\lvert c \rvert\) lives in the numerator of IDF, divided by the document frequency (how many documents contain the term). And do not forget the leading "\(1 +\)" in IDF.

⊛ Drill the why — why a logarithm in IDF, and why does it "decrease"?

The ratio \(\lvert c \rvert / df\) can be huge for a term in 1 document out of a million. A raw ratio would dwarf TF entirely; the \(\log\) compresses that range. As a term appears in more documents, \(df\) grows, the ratio shrinks toward 1, \(\log(1)=0\), and IDF approaches its floor of \(1\). So IDF is large for rare terms and \(\approx 1\) for ubiquitous ones — a smooth decreasing curve. "Inverse" is literal: it is inversely related to how many documents contain the term.

Jazz example (slide 12). 15 jazz biographies from Wikipedia → ~2,000 features after stemming and stop-word removal. The query "Famous jazz saxophonist born in Kansas who played bebop and latin" is itself turned into a TF-IDF vector; cosine similarity ranks the documents: Charlie Parker 0.135 (top), Count Basie 0.119, Dizzie Gillespie 0.086, John Coltrane 0.079… The rare, discriminative term "latin" carries the highest per-term weight.

A term appears 3 times in a 100-word document, and that document is 1 of 50 in the corpus. The term occurs in 5 of those 50 documents. Which statement is correct?

  1. TF = 3/50, because we divide the count by the number of documents in the corpus.
  2. TF = 3/100 (count ÷ document length), and IDF = 1 + log(50/5), which is clearly above 1.
  3. IDF ≈ 1 here, because the term is common enough to be treated like a stop-word.

BM25: saturating frequency and normalizing length

TF grows linearly: a word appearing 100 times gets 100× the weight of appearing once. Why is that unreasonable for relevance ranking, and what would a better curve look like?
Once a word appears, say, 5–10 times, you are already confident the document is about it — the 50th occurrence adds almost nothing. Linear TF over-rewards keyword stuffing and long documents. A better function saturates: it rises quickly at first, then flattens (diminishing returns). BM25 does exactly this, plus a tunable document-length correction. That is why BM25 often beats TF-IDF and, the slides note, sometimes even beats embedding models on retrieval.
BM25 query–document score \[ \mathrm{BM25}(q, d) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathrm{IDF}(q_i)\cdot \frac{ f(q_i, d)\,(k_1 + 1) }{ f(q_i, d) + k_1\!\left(1 - b + b\,\dfrac{\lvert d \rvert}{\mathrm{avgdl}}\right) } \]

Here \(f(q_i,d)\) is the count of keyword \(q_i\) in \(d\), \(\mathrm{avgdl}\) is the average document length, and \(k_1\) (typically \(1.2\) to \(2.0\)) and \(b\) (typically \(0.75\)) are free parameters. The same expression per term \(t_i\) gives a feature weight \(w_{t_i,d}\), so BM25 can also produce a per-document feature vector.

⊛ Drill the why — read the formula's two knobs

\(k_1\) controls saturation. As \(f \to \infty\), the fraction tends to the ceiling \(k_1+1\) — adding more occurrences yields ever-smaller gains. Set \(k_1\) small for fast saturation, large for more linear behavior. \(b\) controls length normalization. With \(b=1\) you fully divide by relative length \(\lvert d\rvert/\mathrm{avgdl}\) (penalizing long documents); with \(b=0\) length is ignored. TF-IDF has neither knob — that is precisely what BM25 adds.

TF-IDF

  • Document feature matrix
  • Linear TF
  • Length norm: divide TF by \(\lvert d\rvert\)
  • No free parameters
  • Strong baseline

BM25

  • Query–document ranking
  • Saturated TF (nonlinear, \(k_1\))
  • Better length norm (\(b\), avgdl)
  • Params \(k_1\!\approx\!1.2\text{–}2.0\), \(b\!\approx\!0.75\)
  • Often beats TF-IDF; sometimes beats embeddings

N-grams, high dimensionality, and classical NLP

N-grams use sequences of adjacent words as terms, recovering a little of the word order BoW threw away. "The quick brown fox jumps" → bi-grams {quick_brown, brown_fox, fox_jumps}, often combined with the 1-grams. The cost: they greatly increase the feature set — sparsity gets worse.

All of stage 1 yields very high-dimensional, sparse features. The toolbox for that: feature selection; fast scalable models (linear Naive Bayes, SVMs; clustering via NMF); the hashing trick (collapse many features into \(n\) hashed buckets); and dimensionality reduction (t-SNE, UMAP).

Classical NLP learns language structure from data and handles context, negation, sarcasm better than BoW. Its best-known tasks: PoS tagging, Named Entity Recognition (NER) with per-entity salience scores, and topic modeling via LDA (per-document topic proportions \(\theta_d\), per-topic word distributions \(\varphi_k\), Dirichlet parameters \(\alpha, \beta\)) and LSA. Status today: mostly replaced by deep learning.

Word embeddings: dense, learned, similarity-aware vectors

stage 2

Why sparse representations hurt, and what we want instead

BoW / n-gram / TF-IDF vectors are huge and mostly zero. Beyond "wasteful," why does that sparsity actively hurt a learning algorithm — and what property do we wish the representation had instead?
Each word gets its own orthogonal dimension, so "cat" and "kitten" are as far apart as "cat" and "democracy" — the geometry encodes no similarity. High dimension + sparsity also means data is spread thin (curse of dimensionality), and sequences of such vectors are even more uninformative. We want the opposite: dense, lower-dimensional, continuous vectors learned from data, where distance captures similarity. That object is an embedding.
Word embedding
A dense vector of real values per word, with dimension much smaller than the vocabulary. Each word becomes a point in "embedding space"; closeness (Euclidean or cosine) means relatedness.
⊛ Drill the why — the analogy "magic" is just vector arithmetic

"Man is to woman as king is to ___?" resolves by offsets:

The canonical analogy\[ [\text{king}] - [\text{man}] + [\text{woman}] \approx [\text{queen}] \]

The vector \([\text{woman}]-[\text{man}]\) encodes the "male → female" direction; adding it to \([\text{king}]\) lands near \([\text{queen}]\). Verb-tense (walking→walked) and country–capital (China→Beijing) relations show up as roughly parallel offset vectors. This emerges because embeddings are trained to reflect context, and words sharing relations share contexts.

How embeddings are constructed: self-supervision

The key trick: don't hand-build the vectors — learn them from a large corpus using a self-supervised task (the labels come for free from the text itself). Two families of task:

  • Predict-from-context: skip-gram and CBOW (below) — guess a focus word from its neighbors, or vice versa.
  • Adversarial / discriminative: swap a word in a 5-gram for a random word and have the model judge whether the 5-gram is real.

The learner can be simple matrix factorization or a more powerful neural network. Either way, the learned embedding becomes a reusable feature vector — transfer learning to other tasks.

word2vec — CBOW and skip-gram

word2vec (Google, 2013, Mikolov et al.) learns one vector per word (term level, not document level). Central idea: a word is defined by its context. Two training framings.

CBOW — predict word from context

  • Input = surrounding context words (each one-hot, length \(V\))
  • Output = the focus word (softmax)
  • Hidden activation = average of context vectors
  • Context order ignored (assumes input independence)
  • Averaging "smooths" ⇒ needs less data

Skip-gram (CSG) — predict context from word

  • Input = the single focus word (one-hot)
  • Output = each context word (presented one by one)
  • Hidden = pass-through (no activation)
  • Easier to implement
  • Better-quality vectors but needs more data
⚠ Do not swap these — the favourite exam trick

CBOW = Context → wOrd, averaging activation, less data. Skip-gram = word → context (it "skips" out to neighbors), pass-through, more data but better vectors. If you remember "CBOW averages (so it's smooth and data-cheap)," you won't flip them.

Numeric example (CBOW, slide 31). \(V=10\), \(N=4\), \(C=3\). The three one-hot context inputs select rows 2, 5, 9 of \(W\):

averaging the selected rows → hidden activation \[ \frac{[0,0,1,0] + [1,0,0,-1] + [1,1,0,0]}{3} = [0.66,\ 0.33,\ 0.33,\ -0.33] \]
⊛ Drill the why — why does CBOW reduce to a lookup table?

A one-hot vector times the matrix \(W\) just selects a row of \(W\) (all other rows are multiplied by 0). So after training, getting a word's embedding is a simple row lookup in \(W\) — the embedding layer is an integer "lookup table" of trainable weights of length \(N\). That is also why order does not matter: averaging selected rows is commutative. The whole context plus the desired focus word is one training example.

✦ ELI-intern — what is actually being learned?

Picture a spreadsheet \(W\) with one row per vocabulary word and \(N\) columns. Initially the rows are random. Training nudges rows so that words appearing in similar contexts drift toward similar rows. The network (softmax output, the second matrix) is just scaffolding to generate a learning signal. When training ends you throw the network away and keep \(W\) — its rows are the word vectors. The hidden-layer size \(N\) = the embedding dimensionality. The whole thing is "an auto-encoder with context": dimensionality reduction guided by which words co-occur.

Good implementations add: random context-word selection, subsampling (skip retraining very frequent words), and negative sampling (update only a few weights per step, not all of \(W'\)). To visualize 300- or 1000-dimensional vectors, project to 2D with a second step of PCA or t-SNE — don't train the embedding in 2D directly.

2

After training a word2vec model, what do you keep to use as word vectors, and why?

  1. The hidden-layer weight matrix \(W\); each word's row is its embedding, and the output layer is discarded.
  2. The output softmax weights, because they produce the probability distribution over words.
  3. The one-hot input vectors, since they already uniquely identify each word.

GloVe, fastText, doc2vec

GloVe

Global Vectors (Pennington et al.). Argues word2vec is suboptimal because it doesn't fully exploit global co-occurrence statistics. Combines skip-gram's analogy strengths with matrix factorization over the global co-occurrence matrix. Output is similar to word2vec.

fastText

Facebook library. Builds a word vector from the vectors of its character n-grams (subwords), stored in a hashtable. Consequence: it can embed any word — even misspelled or made-up ones, which word2vec/GloVe cannot. Still used in industry.

doc2vec / par2vec

Extends word2vec to whole documents: a Paragraph Matrix holds a paragraph-id vector alongside word vectors; these are averaged/concatenated to predict the next word — yielding a document-level embedding.

⊛ Drill the why — why is fastText's OOV ability a big deal?

word2vec/GloVe assign vectors to whole words seen in training. A typo ("teh"), a rare compound, or a brand-new term has no vector — it's simply unknown. fastText composes a word's vector from its substrings ("where" = wh, whe, her, ere, re…), so even an unseen word inherits a sensible vector from its pieces. This handles misspellings, morphology, and concatenations — crucial for noisy, real-world text.

3

You must embed user-generated text full of typos and invented slang. Which model handles out-of-vocabulary words, and on what principle?

  1. GloVe, because it uses global co-occurrence statistics across the whole corpus.
  2. word2vec skip-gram, because it produces higher-quality vectors than CBOW.
  3. fastText, because it composes a word's vector from its character n-grams (subwords).

Generalizing embeddings: categoricals, graphs, and the big idea

An embedding is simply a layer that maps a categorical input to a vector of trainable weights, learned by ordinary SGD. So the idea generalizes far beyond words:

  • Entity / categorical embeddings: for a high-cardinality categorical (e.g. store id, day-of-week), feed only that categorical into a small net → embedding → outcome. One-hot day-of-week needs 6 dims (7−1); an embedding compresses it, reducing overfitting. Jeremy Howard's size heuristic: \(1.6 \cdot \lvert \text{cats} \rvert^{0.56}\).
  • node2vec: embeddings for graph nodes.
  • t-SNE, UMAP, PCA, auto-encoders are all, in this light, ways to create a representation.
◇ Broader context — the thesis statement of the chapter

"The main task of ML and especially deep learning is learning compressed, dense representations that fit the downstream task." Words, documents, categories, graph nodes — same recipe. The chapter also flags a sharp ethical edge: embeddings absorb the biases of their corpus. The WEAT test and the "Man is to Computer Programmer as Woman is to Homemaker" result show learned gender bias baked into the geometry.

Recurrent neural networks: learning from sequences

stage 3

The state idea and the recurrent cell

Embeddings give one vector per word, but a sentence is a sequence of varying length. What's the core problem, and what does an RNN keep around to solve it?
A feed-forward net needs a fixed-size input, but sequences vary in length and order matters. The RNN's idea: feed inputs one at a time and carry a state — a compact summary of everything seen so far that's relevant to the task. At each step the new state is a function of the current input and the previous state, so information flows forward through time. The network performs the same task at every position (hence "recurrent"), with output depending on prior computations.
plain RNN cell — state update and output \[ a^{\langle t\rangle} = \tanh\!\left(W_{ax}\,x^{\langle t\rangle} + W_{aa}\,a^{\langle t-1\rangle} + b_a\right), \qquad \hat{y}^{\langle t\rangle} = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(W_{ya}\,a^{\langle t\rangle} + b_y\right) \]

In the plain RNN the output at each step equals the hidden state (then optionally passed through a softmax). The state at time \(t\) depends on the state at \(t-1\), which depends on \(t-2\), and so on — a chain back to the start.

BPTT and why plain RNNs struggle

⊛ Drill the why — why hidden-state recurrence, and why BPTT?

Why recur on the hidden state (not just on the previous input)? A hidden state lets the network learn what to remember and what to discard across arbitrarily many steps, rather than being limited to a fixed window of raw inputs. Why BPTT? Because the same weights are reused at every time step, the unrolled network is a deep net with shared weights; to train it you backpropagate through the time-unfolded graph — ordinary backprop, just on the unrolled chain, accumulating each weight's gradient across all the steps it was used.

⚠ The catch — RNNs forget

In theory RNNs handle arbitrarily long sequences; in practice they look back only a few steps. The usual neural-net demons return — vanishing gradients — and the impact of the initial state can be severe. An RNN is a nonlinear dynamical system, so weight changes can make it unstable (bifurcations). Remembering "a bracket opened long ago and must be closed" is genuinely hard for a plain RNN.

LSTM and GRU: gating to remember longer

The fix is gating: learn to conditionally set or reset state values based on the current input and previous state. Two designs:

LSTM (1997, Hochreiter & Schmidhuber)

  • Gates: forget \(f_t\), input \(i_t\), output \(o_t\) (each via \(\sigma\)); candidate via \(\tanh\)
  • A cell state \(C_t\) runs along the top as a memory highway
  • Two values passed to next step: cell state \(C_t\) and hidden \(h_t\)
  • Better for long-range dependencies in theory

GRU (2014, Cho et al.)

  • Gates: reset \(r_t\), update \(z_t\) (with a \((1-)\) complement), single \(\tanh\)
  • One value passed to next step: hidden \(h_t\)
  • Trains faster, often better on less data
  • Used at least as often as LSTM now — try both
✦ ELI-intern — what does a "gate" actually do?

A gate is a vector of numbers between 0 and 1 (from a sigmoid \(\sigma\)), multiplied element-wise into another vector. A 0 closes the gate (blocks that value), a 1 opens it (lets it through). The forget gate decides which parts of the old cell memory to erase; the input gate decides which new candidate info to write; the output gate decides what to expose as \(h_t\). Because the cell state \(C_t\) is mostly added to (not repeatedly multiplied by weights), gradients flow back through it without vanishing — that is the whole point. The network learns these gate values, so it learns its own memory policy.

4

Which statement about LSTM vs. GRU is correct?

  1. GRU passes both a cell state and a hidden state; LSTM passes only a hidden state.
  2. LSTM passes two values (cell state \(C_t\) and hidden \(h_t\)); GRU passes only \(h_t\) and tends to train faster.
  3. Both pass two values, but only the GRU uses a forget gate to fight vanishing gradients.

Bidirectional RNN and seq2seq

A bidirectional RNN runs one RNN forward and one backward over the sequence and combines their states per position. This is not cheating by looking at the future — it is legitimate when the whole sequence is already available at inference (e.g. tagging a complete sentence), as opposed to online, left-to-right prediction.

Sequence-to-sequence (seq2seq) pairs an encoder and a decoder:

  • Encoder (LSTM/GRU) reads the whole input and compresses it into a fixed-length context vector — a summary of the source's meaning. (Early work used only the encoder's last state.)
  • Decoder (LSTM/GRU) is initialized from the context vector and generates the output sequence one word at a time (e.g. "I like cats" → "J'aime les chats").

Context-vector length = number of encoder units (e.g. 256, 512, 1024); an embedding layer sits on top of the input tokens.

Attention: stop squeezing everything into one vector

stage 3 → 4
A long sentence must pass through a single fixed-length context vector to the decoder. Why is that a fundamental problem, and what would you let the decoder do instead?
It is unreasonable to cram all the information of a long, possibly 50-word sentence into one vector of, say, 512 numbers — the early words get washed out (RNNs forget; the encoder's last state over-represents the end). Translating long sentences stays problematic. The fix: let the decoder, at each output step, look back at all the encoder's hidden states and focus on the few most relevant to the word it's generating now. That selective focusing is attention.

What attention changes about seq2seq

Two differences from plain seq2seq:

  1. The encoder passes all hidden states to the decoder, not just the last one.
  2. Before producing each output word, the decoder does an extra step: score every encoder hidden state, softmax the scores, then take the score-weighted sum of the states — amplifying relevant words, drowning out the rest. (Trick that also helps: feeding the source reversed shortens the path from decoder to early words.)
⊛ Drill the why — Query, Key, Value in plain words

Think of a soft dictionary lookup. The Query is "what am I looking for right now?" = the decoder's current hidden state. The Keys are "what can I match against?" = the encoder hidden states. The match score (Query · Key) says how relevant each key is. Softmax turns scores into weights. The Values (also the encoder states) are what you actually retrieve — you return a weighted blend of values, weighted by relevance. So attention = a differentiable, soft lookup.

attention as a soft lookup (decoder step) \[ \text{weights} = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q^{\top}K}{\sqrt{n}}\right), \qquad \text{context} = \text{weights}\times V \]

Worked step (slide 64). Encoder states scored 13, 9, 9 → softmax 0.96, 0.02, 0.02 → multiply each state by its weight → sum → the context vector for this decoder step. The alignment heat-maps (English→French) come out roughly diagonal, with sensible reordering for phrases like "European Economic Area" ↔ "zone économique européenne".

Many score functions exist; the one that wins is scaled dot-product (Vaswani 2017):

scaled dot-product score \[ \mathrm{score}(s_t, h_i) = \frac{s_t^{\top} h_i}{\sqrt{n}}, \qquad n = \text{source hidden-state dimension} \]
⊛ Drill the why — why divide by \(\sqrt{n}\)?

A dot product of two \(n\)-dimensional vectors sums \(n\) products, so its magnitude grows with \(n\). Large scores push softmax into a near one-hot regime where gradients vanish. Dividing by \(\sqrt{n}\) keeps the variance of the score roughly constant regardless of dimension, so softmax stays in a well-behaved range and training is stable. It is a numerical-stability fix, not a semantic one.

◇ Broader context — the pivotal question

With attention, we no longer compress the whole source into one fixed vector — the network attends to different parts at each output step. The slides then ask the question that launches the next era: "Maybe we can do away with recurrence altogether now?" If attention can connect any output to any input directly, do we even need the slow, forgetful recurrent chain? The answer is the Transformer.

The Transformer: attention is all you need

stage 4

Self-attention

In seq2seq attention, Q came from the decoder and K, V from the encoder. What changes in self-attention, and why is that the key to dropping recurrence?
In self-attention, Q, K, and V all come from the same set of tokens (the input sequence itself). Each token asks "which other tokens should I look at to understand myself?" and updates its representation as a weighted blend of all tokens. Because every token can directly reach every other token in one step — no chain to walk — you no longer need recurrence to move information across the sequence. And it parallelizes: all positions are processed at once.

Mechanism per token:

  1. From each token's embedding, compute three vectors via three weight matrices: \(Q = X W^Q\), \(K = X W^K\), \(V = X W^V\). All from the same input set — that is what makes it self-attention.
  2. Compare each Query with all Keys → attention scores.
  3. Softmax the scores → attention weights.
  4. Take the weighted sum of Values → the token's new representation.
self-attention in matrix form \[ Q = X W^{Q},\quad K = X W^{K},\quad V = X W^{V}, \qquad Z = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{Q K^{\top}}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V \]

Stacked decoder blocks gradually refine each token's representation by letting it repeatedly look at the others.

⊛ Drill the why — the single most-tested contrast

"Attention mixes information BETWEEN tokens; the feed-forward network processes each token INDIVIDUALLY." Self-attention is the only place where one token's information reaches another. After attention, every token is pushed through the same small feed-forward network independently — no cross-token interaction there. So a Transformer block alternates: mix (attention), then think per-token (FFNN), with residual (skip) connections and layer norm around each.

Masking — why GPT can generate

⊛ Drill the why — masked self-attention

For generation, a token must only attend to previous tokens — if it could peek at future tokens during training, it would "cheat" and never learn to predict them. So the attention matrix is masked: future positions are blocked out (set to \(-\infty\) before softmax). The slides put it directly: "This is why GPT can generate text." A decoder-only Transformer with masked self-attention learns next-token prediction honestly.

Multi-head attention and positional encoding

Multi-head attention: instead of one set of \(Q/K/V\) matrices, use several (multiple "representation subspaces"). Each head can focus on a different relationship (syntax, coreference, …). Run them in parallel, then concatenate all heads \(Z_0 \dots Z_h\) and multiply by a learned matrix \(W^O\) to mix them back into one \(Z\) sent to the FFNN.

⊛ Drill the why — why does self-attention need positional encoding at all?

Self-attention is a set operation — it sums over tokens with weights, and a sum doesn't care about order. "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" would look identical. So we add a positional-encoding vector to each token embedding (built from sinusoids of varying frequency), injecting "where in the sequence am I." Note it is added, not concatenated, and added before the attention layers. This is the explicit replacement for the order information that recurrence gave us for free.

The output head

At the top, the same linear layer is applied to every position's final representation to produce vocabulary logits:

output projection to vocabulary \[ \text{logits} = X\, W_{\text{vocab}} \]

During training, every position predicts its next token at once — full parallelism. During inference, you use only the last position's prediction, append the sampled token to the input, and run again — autoregressive generation.

6

Why must a Transformer add positional encodings to its input embeddings?

  1. Self-attention is permutation-invariant, so without position info word order would be lost.
  2. To normalize the embedding magnitudes so softmax does not saturate.
  3. To mask out future tokens so the model cannot cheat during generation.
7

In a Transformer block, which component lets one token's information influence another's representation?

  1. The position-wise feed-forward network, which combines all token vectors.
  2. Layer normalization, which averages across the sequence.
  3. Self-attention; the feed-forward network processes each token independently.

GPT, RLHF, and the post-Transformer frontier

stage 5

GPT: a big decoder-only Transformer

GPT (Generative Pretrained Transformer) is a decoder-only Transformer with masked self-attention, scaled up and trained on text completion given a prompt (next-token prediction). Implementation tricks: Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) tokenization and sparse self-attention. Across generations the architecture stayed essentially the same; what scaled was data, plus tool calling, reasoning tokens, multi-modal input/output, better positional encoding, mixture of experts.

◇ The LLM family tree (slide 74)

From the common root (word2vec / GloVe / fastText), three branches: encoder-only (BERT, RoBERTa, ELECTRA, DeBERTa — understanding/classification), encoder-decoder (T5, BART, UL2 — seq2seq tasks), and decoder-only (GPT, LLaMA, PaLM, Claude, BLOOM — generation). The decoder-only trunk is the dominant one today.

RLHF — aligning to human preference

⊛ Drill the why — why can't we just write a loss for "be helpful"?

Desirable attributes (helpful, honest, not toxic) are intractable to express as a differentiable loss function. So models are still trained with a simple next-token-prediction loss, then patched. People define proxy metrics like BLEU and ROUGE to approximate human preference. RLHF goes further: collect human feedback on generated text and use it as the performance signal — even as a loss to optimize. This aligns a general-corpus model with complex human values. It was applied on top of GPT-3 (InstructGPT) and is part of ChatGPT.

The Transformer bottleneck and what comes after

A Transformer recomputes attention over the whole sequence for every new token. Why is that costly, and how does an RNN avoid it?
To generate token \(t\), self-attention compares the new token to all previous tokens; doing this for each of \(L\) generated tokens gives roughly \(L^2\) work — it grows quadratically and recomputes already-generated tokens. An RNN only needs the previous hidden state plus the current input, so generation scales linearly in length and (in theory) has unbounded context. The price RNNs pay: they tend to forget (one state) and are slow to train because of BPTT. This trade-off is exactly what SSMs try to win on both sides.

Named non-Transformer alternatives:

  • State Space Models (SSM): a "state space" = the minimal set of variables fully describing a system. SSMs model the state and predict the next state from input — linear-time sequence modeling. Examples: Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023, selective state spaces) and xLSTM (Beck et al., 2024).
  • Diffusion LLMs: start from corrupted/random tokens and denoise into coherent text. Non-autoregressive ⇒ parallel token prediction, but quality historically lags Transformers; sometimes used as a refinement layer on top.
◇ Broader context — agents and tooling

An LLM agent "runs tools in a loop to achieve a goal" (Simon Willison). The wider ecosystem: Gensim (word2vec/GloVe/fastText, LDA), spaCy, NLTK, Hugging Face, vaderSentiment (social-media sentiment), and for local inference llama.cpp (GGUF models) and Ollama. The throughline: nearly everything modern is still a Transformer — but the field is actively probing past its \(L^2\) wall.

8

Why is RNN inference said to scale better than Transformer inference for very long generated sequences?

  1. RNNs use multi-head attention, which is cheaper than single-head attention.
  2. An RNN needs only the previous hidden state and current input per step (linear), while a Transformer recomputes attention over the whole sequence (\(\approx L^2\)).
  3. RNNs never forget, so they need no positional encoding and skip most computation.
9

What is GPT's architecture and training objective, at the core?

  1. An encoder-only Transformer trained to fill in masked words anywhere in the sentence.
  2. An encoder-decoder Transformer trained on translation pairs with RLHF as the base loss.
  3. A decoder-only Transformer with masked self-attention, trained on next-token prediction (text completion).
