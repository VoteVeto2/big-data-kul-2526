The core problem — "Can we have it all?"

Everything in this chapter exists to answer one frustration about single models. Start here, because the rest is a series of clever answers to it.

Restate the problem in your own words: a single decision tree, grown deeper and deeper, behaves how on training data versus unseen data — and why is that a problem we cannot fix by just "tuning the tree"?
As the tree grows, training accuracy keeps climbing (toward ~0.86 in the slide) because the tree memorizes individual points. But test accuracy peaks early (~0.75 around 10 nodes) and then drops (~0.69) — the deep tree fits noise. "Great accuracy on train data, but worthless on unseen data." We can fix it with pruning / early stopping, but that buys robustness by sacrificing peak accuracy — you cap the tree at ~20 nodes and lose the high-end performance. The open question: is there a way to get robustness and high accuracy at once? That is what ensembles deliver.

The problem in detail

A model can fail in two opposite directions:

Overfitting (too complex)
A very deep tree carves jagged decision regions that hug individual training points. Train accuracy soars; test accuracy collapses. The model learned noise, not signal.
Underfitting (too simple)
A single straight logistic boundary cannot isolate a red cluster sitting inside a blue region — one linear cut is too rigid for a non-linear truth.

The single-model fixes (pruning, early stopping) only let you slide along this trade-off. They cannot break it. The recurring "WHY" of the whole lecture:

Drill the why

Why can't a single model "have it all"? Because complexity is a single dial: turn it up and you reduce bias but inflate variance; turn it down and the reverse. Pruning just picks a point on that one dial. Ensembles add a second mechanism — averaging many diverse models cancels their variance (bagging), or chaining many weak models cancels their bias (boosting). That second lever is what no single tree can offer.

The "wisdom of the crowd" intuition

The jelly-bean-jar: ask hundreds of people to guess the number of beans. Each guess is noisy — some too high, some too low. But the mean of all guesses (≈ 487) lands remarkably close to the truth. The errors are roughly independent, so they cancel. Ensembles formalize this: many imperfect models, aggregated, beat any single one — provided their errors are diverse.

ELI-intern

One expert can be confidently wrong. A committee of differently-biased experts, each voting, rarely is — because for a mistake to survive the vote, a majority would all have to be wrong in the same way. Diversity is the whole game. A committee of identical clones is no better than one clone.

Why it matters

This single tension — accuracy vs. robustness — is why random forests and gradient boosting exist, why they dominate Kaggle on tabular data, and why "just use one big model" is rarely the answer for structured data. Hold onto it; every later section is a different way of cancelling either variance or bias.

  1. High bias — it is too simple to capture the pattern.
  2. High variance — it memorized training noise and fails to generalize.
  3. Irreducible error — no model could do better here.

Combining models: rules, voting, experts, stacking, smoothing

Before the two big algorithms (bagging, boosting), the lecture surveys the basic combination machinery: given several trained models, how do you fuse their outputs?

Combination rules (algebraic)

Take the probability outputs of two models and combine them arithmetically, then find a new cutoff:

  • Min — take the smaller of the two probabilities (conservative: both must be confident).
  • Max — take the larger (lenient: either model's confidence suffices).
  • Mean — average the two (balanced).
The cutoff trap

After combining, the raw 0.50 threshold is no longer valid — each rule needs its own re-tuned cutoff. In the worked example: Min → 0.50, Max → 0.78, Mean → 0.52. With those, Mean classifies all 6 instances correctly, while Max (0.78) still mislabels a true "Yes" of 0.54 as "No". No algebraic rule is universally best.

Voting (majority & weighted)

Instead of combining probabilities, combine discrete decisions: count votes, the majority class wins.

  • Majority voting — 6 models, 4 say "yes", 2 say "no" → "yes" wins, 4 to 2.
  • Weighted voting — give trusted models more votes. If Model 4 carries 5 votes and one ally votes with it (5 + 1 = 6) against 4 others saying "yes", then "no" wins.
Drill the why

Why prefer voting over algebraic combination? Because voting is less sensitive to the underlying probability distributions. Algebraic rules operate on raw probabilities, so they assume the models are calibrated and demand a freshly tuned cutoff. Voting only needs each model's decision — no calibration, no new cutoff. But there's a catch: determining the weights for weighted voting is itself a learning problem (meta-learning).

Mixture of experts (Jacobs/Jordan, 1991)

Train several "experts," each specializing in a different region of input space. A separate gating network decides whom to trust for a given input \( x \), producing the combination weights.

Mixture of experts\[ H(x) = \sum_{i=1}^{T} w_i \cdot h_i(x, \theta_i) \]

The gating network is usually trained with the Expectation–Maximization (EM) algorithm. The weights \( w_i \) are input-dependent: a different mix of experts is trusted for different \( x \).

Stacking / stacked generalization (Wolpert, 1992)

A two-tier ensemble. Tier-1 base classifiers \( C_1 \ldots C_T \) are trained; then a Tier-2 meta-classifier \( C^{*} \) is trained on the outputs of the Tier-1 models (optionally plus the original features).

Drill the why

What does the meta-classifier actually learn? Whether the training data have been properly learned by the base models. If classifier \( C_3 \) reliably mislabels a particular region of feature space, the Tier-2 model learns to correct that — "when \( C_3 \) says X here, flip it." Stacking learns the error pattern of its base models.

Smoothing (blend by \( \lambda \))

Blend a simple and a complex model by a convex combination:

Smoothing blend\[ \text{Ensemble} = \lambda \cdot (\text{logistic regression}) + (1 - \lambda) \cdot (\text{complex model}), \quad \lambda \in [0,1] \]

A pure complex model has a jagged, overfit boundary; pure logistic regression has a smooth but underfit one. The blend keeps the flexible shape of the complex model but smooths it toward the simple model — pulling it back from the noise.

Bagging — bootstrap aggregating

The first of the two great ensemble families. Simple, old, surprisingly powerful — and the foundation of random forests.

Restate it: bagging trains many models. What exactly is different about the data each model sees, and what single property of the ensemble are we trying to improve by doing that?
Each model is trained on a bootstrap replica — a sample drawn with replacement from the full training set, so each replica is a slightly different, randomly-perturbed view of the data. By feeding each model a different view, we make the models diverse: they make different errors. When we aggregate (majority vote or average), those independent errors cancel, which reduces variance and makes the ensemble robust to overfitting and noise. The single property we improve is variance.

How it works

  1. Draw multiple training subsets randomly, with replacement, from the full set (bootstrap samples).
  2. Train one classifier of the same type per subset.
  3. Combine via majority vote (original publication) of their decisions.

Why it needs diversity — and unstable learners

Drill the why

Why do bootstraps alone sometimes give weak diversity, and what fixes it? Because bootstrap samples drawn from the same dataset overlap substantially — roughly 63% of points appear in any given replica — so the models can end up quite similar. The cure is to inject extra randomness: subsample features per model, subsample data, and crucially use unstable base learners — models whose output changes a lot under small data perturbations (decision trees are the classic example). A stable learner (like a linear model) would barely change across bootstraps, giving you near-identical clones and no benefit.

Output formulas

Majority voting\[ p_{\text{ens}}(y=1 \mid x) = \frac{\bigl|\{\, c \in C : p_c(y=1 \mid x) \ge 0.5 \,\}\bigr|}{|C|} \]
Averaged probability\[ p_{\text{ens}}(y=1 \mid x) = \frac{1}{|C|}\sum_{c \in C} p_c(y=1 \mid x) \]

For regression, simply average the predictions. Note scikit-learn combines classifiers by averaging probabilities, not by per-class voting — contrary to the original Breiman publication. For large ensembles these "virtually guarantee the same outcome," because averaging many probabilities and taking the majority vote tend to agree.

Why it matters

Bagging is the cleanest demonstration of variance reduction: it's parallel, embarrassingly easy to implement, and gives you a free validation estimate (next). Its best use is wrapping high-variance, low-bias learners — exactly the deep trees that overfit on their own.

Out-of-bag (OOB) validation

Because each bootstrap is drawn with replacement, each replica leaves out roughly a third of the instances. Those left-out points are "unseen" by that model — so they form a natural hold-out.

Drill the why

How do you compute an OOB estimate for instance \( i \)? Aggregate predictions for \( i \) using only the models whose bootstrap sample did not contain \( i \), then compare to its true label. You get a generalization estimate "for free" — no separate split needed. But the lecture is emphatic: OOB is a good initial check, not a substitute for an independent test set.

The 100% bootstrap trap

A bootstrap of size 100% does NOT mean "use every instance." Drawing \( N \) times with replacement from \( N \) instances leaves out, in the limit, \( e^{-1} \approx 0.368 \) of them — about 37% never get picked. Those become the OOB set.

Where does the ~37% come from? (the math)

The chance one specific instance is missed in a single draw is \( 1 - \tfrac{1}{N} \). Across \( N \) independent draws:

\[ \Bigl(1 - \frac{1}{N}\Bigr)^{N} \xrightarrow{\;N \to \infty\;} e^{-1} \approx 0.368 \]

So ~37% of instances are out-of-bag, leaving ~63% in-bag.

Random forests

Bagging's most famous child — and the single most exam-trapped concept in the chapter.

Before reading: a random forest is "bagged decision trees plus one more source of randomness." What is that extra source, and — critically — how often is it applied?
The extra source is random feature subset selection: at each candidate split, the tree considers only a random subset of \( M \) features (not all). The critical detail and classic exam trap: this is sampled at every split, a fresh random subset each time — NOT once per tree. So a random forest has two sources of randomness: (1) the bootstrap of rows, and (2) the per-split feature subsampling.

The two sources of randomness

  • Bagging randomness — each tree sees a random bootstrap of the rows.
  • Feature randomness — at each split, consider only a random subset of \( M \) features, resampled at every split.
Drill the why

Why subsample features at every split instead of once per tree? If one or two features are very strong predictors, plain bagged trees would all split on them near the root — making the trees highly correlated, which defeats variance reduction (correlated errors don't cancel). By forcing each split to choose from a random feature subset, sometimes the dominant feature isn't even available, so the trees are pushed to explore different structures. Result: decorrelated, diverse trees → much better variance reduction. Doing it once per tree would not break the correlation deep inside the trees.

History & why it replaced pruning

First random decision forests: Tin Kam Ho (1995), the random subspace method. Extended by Leo Breiman and Adele Cutler, who trademarked "Random Forests" in 2006, combining Breiman's bagging with Ho's (and Amit & Geman's) random feature selection to build variance-controlled trees.

Why it matters — no more pruning

Plain trees overfit and need pruning. Random forests control variance by design through the two randomness sources, so pruning is no longer necessary. (You may still prune/early-stop to speed training, but it's not needed for generalization.) This is the practical payoff: little configuration, little preprocessing, interaction effects captured "for free."

Hyperparameters

Number of trees
No overfitting risk from adding trees — use plenty. (Validation accuracy plateaus, it does not degrade.)
Tree depth
No pruning necessary; optional pruning/early stopping only to speed things up.
Bootstrap size
Can be 100% (which, recall, still leaves out ~37% via with-replacement sampling). Lower is fine with enough data; the key is enough trees.
\( M \) = feature subset size
Could be 1, 2, or all (then it's "default bagging"). Heuristics below.
Feature subset heuristic (N = number of features)\[ M = \left\lfloor \tfrac{N}{3} \right\rfloor \;(\ge 1)\ \text{for regression}, \qquad M = \left\lfloor \sqrt{N} \right\rfloor \ \text{for classification} \]

Example with 16 features: classification \( M = \lfloor\sqrt{16}\rfloor = 4 \); regression \( M = \lfloor 16/3 \rfloor = 5 \). (Alternatively, choose \( M \) by cross-validation.) Do not swap the two heuristics — that's a favorite trap.

Extensions

Extra Randomized Trees (Extra-Trees)
Adds yet more randomness: also consider a random subset of possible splitting points, not just a random feature subset. More bias, even less variance, faster.
Completely randomized trees
Maximize diversity by building fully random trees (Liu et al., 2005), useful in some downstream applications.
Exam traps clustered here

① Feature randomness is sampled at every split, not once per tree. ② Random forests use two sources of randomness; plain bagging only one. ③ Bootstrap 100% ≠ all instances (~37% out). ④ Random forests need no pruning. ⑤ More trees does not overfit. ⑥ \( \lfloor N/3 \rfloor \) regression, \( \lfloor\sqrt{N}\rfloor \) classification — don't swap. ⑦ Extra-Trees randomize the split points too.

Boosting — the general idea

The second great family. Where bagging builds models in parallel to add diversity, boosting builds them sequentially to add cooperation.

Restate the contrast: boosting also builds an ensemble of models, but two things differ fundamentally from bagging. What are they, and what is each new model trying to do?
(1) No bootstrapping — boosting reweights the existing instances rather than resampling them. (2) Sequential, not parallel — each new model is added one at a time, and each one specifically aims to correct the mistakes of the ensemble so far: misclassified points get heavier weight, so the next learner focuses on them. The idea is cooperation between classifiers (each fixes the last), whereas bagging's idea was diversity.

How it works

  1. Assign a weight to each instance (start uniform).
  2. Train a model on the weighted data.
  3. Reweight the data based on its mistakes — misclassified points get heavier weight.
  4. Train the next model on the reweighted data; combine via weighted voting; repeat.
Why it matters

Boosting decreases bias, so it's best for low-variance, high-bias (simple) base learners — the mirror image of bagging. Chain enough weak, simple learners, each cleaning up the last, and you get a strong, low-bias ensemble. The price: it's sequential (less parallelizable) and it can overfit — "overboosting."

AdaBoost — adaptive boosting

Freund & Schapire, 1996. The first practical boosting algorithm — and the one whose mechanics you must be able to reproduce.

In AdaBoost, after each weak learner is trained, two numbers get updated: a per-instance weight and a per-learner weight. Which way does each move for a hard instance / an accurate learner?
Instance weights: misclassified ("hard") instances get their weight increased, so the next learner pays more attention to them. Learner weight \( \alpha_t \): a more accurate learner (low error \( \varepsilon_t \)) gets a larger \( \alpha_t \), so its vote counts more in the final combination. Accurate learners earn louder votes; hard instances earn more attention.

The algorithm

Labels are \( y \in \{-1, +1\} \). Initialize uniform instance weights \( D_1(i) = 1/N \), so the first round is just a normal classifier.

for t = 1 to T:
    h_t = train weak learner on data weighted by D_t
    eps_t = sum of D_t(i) over all i misclassified by h_t   # weighted error
    if eps_t > 0.5: T = t-1; return                          # learner worse than random -> stop
    alpha_t = 0.5 * ln( (1 - eps_t) / eps_t )                # learner's vote weight
    for i = 1..N:
        D_{t+1}(i) = D_t(i) * exp( -alpha_t * h_t(x_i) * y_i )
    normalize D_{t+1} to sum to 1
output H(x) = sign( sum_t alpha_t * h_t(x) )
Learner weight\[ \alpha_t = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(\frac{1 - \varepsilon_t}{\varepsilon_t}\right) \]
Weight update & final output\[ D_{t+1}(i) = D_t(i)\, e^{-\alpha_t\, h_t(x_i)\, y_i}, \qquad H(x) = \operatorname{sign}\!\left(\sum_{t=1}^{T} \alpha_t\, h_t(x)\right) \]
Drill the why — read the update term

Look at \( h_t(x_i)\, y_i \) in the exponent. If the prediction is correct, \( h_t(x_i) \) and \( y_i \) share a sign, so the product is \( +1 \): the exponent is \( -\alpha_t \), and the weight shrinks. If wrong, the product is \( -1 \): the exponent is \( +\alpha_t \), and the weight grows. That single sign trick is how AdaBoost automatically up-weights the points it keeps getting wrong. And \( \alpha_t \) scales the effect by the learner's reliability.

Why the stop at \( \varepsilon_t \gt 0.5 \), and a numeric feel for \( \alpha_t \)

\( \alpha_t = \tfrac12\ln\!\frac{1-\varepsilon_t}{\varepsilon_t} \). If \( \varepsilon_t = 0.25 \) → \( \alpha_t = \tfrac12\ln 3 \approx 0.549 \) (a confident, well-weighted learner). If \( \varepsilon_t = 0.5 \) → \( \alpha_t = \tfrac12\ln 1 = 0 \): a coin-flip learner contributes nothing. If \( \varepsilon_t \gt 0.5 \) the log goes negative and the learner is worse than random — AdaBoost stops (sets \( T \leftarrow t-1 \) and returns).

The statistical view (Friedman et al.)

AdaBoost can be re-read as an additive logistic regression model that optimizes an exponential loss. It produces an additive model \( F(x) = \sum_{m=1}^{M} c_m f_m(x) \). Two flavors:

Discrete AdaBoost

Fit \( f_m(x) \in \{-1, 1\} \). Set \( c_m = \log\!\frac{1-e_m}{e_m} \); update \( w_i \leftarrow w_i\,\exp[c_m\,\mathbf{1}_{y_i \ne f_m(x_i)}] \); output \( \operatorname{sign}[\sum c_m f_m(x)] \).

Real AdaBoost

Fit a probability estimate \( p_m(x) \in [0,1] \); set \( f_m(x) = \tfrac12\log\!\frac{p_m(x)}{1-p_m(x)} \in \mathbb{R} \); update \( w_i \leftarrow w_i\,\exp[-y_i f_m(x_i)] \); output \( \operatorname{sign}[\sum f_m(x)] \).

AdaBoost's weakness

Because misclassified points are repeatedly up-weighted, noisy data and outliers get amplified — AdaBoost keeps throwing learners at points that are genuinely unlearnable, which hurts generalization. This is part of why too many rounds cause "overboosting."

A weak learner in round \( t \) achieves a weighted error \( \varepsilon_t = 0.5 \). What happens?

  1. It receives the maximum vote weight, since 0.5 is the target error.
  2. Its weight \( \alpha_t = 0 \) — it contributes nothing to the final ensemble.
  3. Its instances are all reset to uniform weights.

In the update \( D_{t+1}(i) = D_t(i)\,e^{-\alpha_t h_t(x_i) y_i} \), an instance that the learner correctly classifies gets its weight…

  1. increased, so the ensemble keeps focusing on it.
  2. left unchanged until normalization.
  3. decreased, because the exponent becomes \( -\alpha_t \).

Gradient Boosting Machines (GBM)

Friedman's generalization: what if we want any loss, not just AdaBoost's exponential one? The answer reframes boosting as gradient descent — in function space.

Restate the motivation: AdaBoost reweights instances to chase mistakes. Gradient boosting takes a different angle — instead of reweighting, what does each new learner directly try to predict, and why does that let us use any loss?
Each new learner directly tries to predict the residuals — the errors \( y - F_m(x) \) of the ensemble so far — and the ensemble is updated to correct them. More generally, it fits the negative gradient of the loss with respect to the model's raw output. Because you only ever need that gradient, you can plug in any differentiable loss (MSE, LogLoss, etc.) and the same machinery works. Residual-fitting is just the special case where the loss is MSE.

Residuals = negative MSE gradient

For squared-error loss, fitting residuals and fitting the negative gradient are the same thing:

MSE loss and its gradient\[ L_{\text{MSE}} = \frac{1}{n}\bigl(y - F(x)\bigr)^2, \qquad -\frac{\partial L_{\text{MSE}}}{\partial F} = \frac{2}{n}\bigl(y - F(x)\bigr) = \frac{2}{n}\, h_m(x) \]

So the residual \( h_m(x) = y - F_m(x) \) is the negative gradient (up to the constant \( 2/n \)). Gradient boosting fits the next weak decision tree to that residual.

Drill the why — "functional gradient descent"

Why call it gradient descent "in function space"? Ordinary gradient descent nudges parameters in the direction that lowers the loss. Gradient boosting nudges the whole function \( F \): the negative gradient tells you, at each data point, which direction \( F \) should move to lower the loss. A weak tree is then fit to approximate that direction, and you take a step. Residual-fitting for MSE is just the most intuitive instance of this general recipe.

Generalizing to any loss

Mean squared error\[ \text{MSE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 \]
Binary cross-entropy\[ \text{LogLoss} = -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\bigl[\, y_i \log p_i + (1 - y_i)\log(1 - p_i)\,\bigr] \]
Multiclass cross-entropy\[ \text{MLogLoss} = -\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\sum_{j=1}^{m} y_{ij}\log p_{ij} \]
ELI-intern — the "too correct" quirk

Plot loss against \( yF \) (positive = correct, negative = error). Most losses (exponential, log-likelihood) shrink monotonically as \( yF \) grows — being more correct is always rewarded. But squared error \( (y-F)^2 \) starts rising again once \( yF \gt 1 \): it actually penalizes predictions that are "too correct." That's why squared-error loss is a poor fit for classification — it punishes confident right answers, which is exactly backwards.

Pitfalls

GBM is sequential → less parallelizable than bagging. It can overfit ("overboosting"), so it needs regularization (learning rate, tree depth limits, etc.). That regularization is precisely what the XGBoost family adds.

10

"In gradient boosting, each new learner always fits the raw residuals \( y - F(x) \)." The most precise correction is:

  1. Correct — it always fits raw residuals, for every loss.
  2. It fits the negative gradient of the loss; residual-fitting is the MSE special case.
  3. It fits the negative gradient, and MSE is the only loss it cannot use.

Extreme gradient boosting: XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost

The workhorses that win Kaggle on tabular data. They keep GBM's idea but improve how the loss is optimized and add regularization.

XGBoost is "GBM but better" in two concrete ways. One is about how it reads the loss surface, the other about controlling complexity. What are they?
(1) Second-order optimization: it uses both first and second derivatives of the loss — the gradient and the Hessian — which gives more information about the curvature of the loss and lets it reach the minimum faster and more stably than first-order GBM. (2) Regularization (L1 / L2) on model complexity — over tree depth or number of leaf nodes — so the objective becomes loss + a penalty for tree complexity, improving generalization.

The two improvements

Second-order optimization
Uses second partial derivatives (the Hessian, alongside the gradient/Jacobian). Knowing the curvature, not just the slope, lets the optimizer take better-sized steps toward the loss minimum — faster training.
Regularization (L1 or L2)
A penalty on complexity — tree depth or number of leaves. The objective combines loss + simplicity of the trees, which directly fights overboosting.
XGBoost-style objective (schematic)\[ \text{Objective} = \underbrace{\sum_i L(y_i, \hat{y}_i)}_{\text{fit the data}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_k \Omega(f_k)}_{\text{penalize tree complexity}} \]
Drill the why

Why does second-order info help? First-order gradient descent only knows the slope of the loss at the current point — it must guess a step size. The Hessian tells you the curvature, so you know how quickly the slope is changing and can choose a near-optimal step directly (a Newton-like update). Fewer, better-aimed steps → faster, more stable convergence than plain GBM.

ELI-intern — level-wise vs leaf-wise

Level-wise (XGBoost) grows the tree one full level at a time — balanced, conservative, stable. Leaf-wise (LightGBM) always splits whichever single leaf would reduce loss the most — it chases the biggest gain, so it grows deep, lopsided trees fast and is extremely accurate, but overfits more easily if you don't cap depth. Symmetric/oblivious (CatBoost) forces every node at the same depth to use the same split — very regular, fast to evaluate, naturally regularized.

Picking guide

Mostly numerical tabular data → start with XGBoost. Very large dataset where speed matters → LightGBM. Many categorical features and you want minimal preprocessing → CatBoost (native categoricals + ordered boosting reduce target leakage, excellent on small/medium data with many categoricals).

What does XGBoost use that plain GBM does not?

  1. Bootstrap sampling of the rows for each tree.
  2. Second-order information (gradient + Hessian) plus L1/L2 regularization.
  3. A gating network to weight each tree.

Boosting refinements: DART, interaction & monotonic constraints

Three practical add-ons that address specific failure modes of boosted trees.

DART — dropouts meet boosted trees

(Vinayak & Gilad-Bachrach, 2015.) Borrows dropout from deep neural nets and applies it to boosted trees.

Drill the why

What problem does DART solve? XGBoost typically combines a huge number of trees with a small learning rate. In that setting, trees added early become dominant and trees added late become nearly irrelevant (over-specialization). By randomly dropping some existing trees when computing the next one, DART forces later trees to contribute meaningfully — improving results in some situations.

Feature interaction constraints

Variables that appear together on a single traversal path are interacting: a child node's condition is predicated on its parent's. When tree depth exceeds 1, many variables interact merely to shave training loss — and the tree may capture a spurious (noise) relationship that won't generalize. An interaction constraint lets you declare which variables are allowed to interact and which are not.

Monotonic relationship constraints

Force a feature to have a monotonic effect on the prediction — always increasing, or always decreasing.

Why it matters — domain knowledge

Sometimes the acceptable form of a model is fixed by domain rules: predicted risk should only rise with a given feature, never wiggle. Without the constraint, a tree fits noisy up-and-down curves; with it, you get clean step-like monotone trends that honor the rule and generalize better. This is as much about trust and compliance as accuracy.

Bagging vs. boosting through the bias–variance lens

This is the conceptual spine of the chapter — and the single richest source of true/false exam traps. Get this table into long-term memory.

Without looking: bagging reduces ___ and suits ___ base models; boosting reduces ___ and suits ___ base models. Fill the four blanks and name one algorithm for each.
Bagging reduces variance and suits high-variance, low-bias (complex) base models — e.g. random forest over deep trees. Boosting reduces bias and suits low-variance, high-bias (simple) base models — e.g. AdaBoost / GBM / XGBoost over weak learners. Mnemonic: Bagging = diversity (parallel); Boosting = cooperation (sequential).

Drill the why — the empirical curves (slide 44)

Why does a random forest's validation accuracy flatten while AdaBoost's declines? Adding trees to a random forest just averages in more diverse, decorrelated estimates — variance keeps falling, validation flattens (~0.91) and never degrades, even at 5000 trees. Adding rounds to AdaBoost keeps pushing the ensemble to fit ever-harder (often noisy) points — train accuracy climbs to ~0.96, but validation peaks early (~0.92) then declines (~0.89). That's overboosting: more cooperation eventually means fitting noise.

The reversal trap

The examiner's favorite move is to swap the bias/variance assignments: "bagging reduces bias, boosting reduces variance" is FALSE. Equally, "bagging suits simple models, boosting suits complex models" is FALSE. Lock in: baGGing → variance → complex; booSTing → bias → simple.

  3. Boosting reduces variance and suits complex high-variance models.
15

Why can bagging be parallelized while boosting fundamentally cannot (across models)?

  1. Each boosting model depends on the previous one's output (reweighting / residuals), so they must run in sequence.
  2. Boosting uses much larger bootstrap samples that don't fit in parallel memory.
  3. Boosting models are trained on disjoint bootstraps that cannot be merged.

Deep learning for tabular data — can it beat the trees?

The closing debate. Short answer: mostly "no(t really)" — with one emerging exception.

Tabular data is unlike images or text in ways that favor tree ensembles. List two structural reasons deep nets struggle on tables, and one reason trees thrive.
Deep nets struggle because tabular data has no strong spatial structure (so CNN priors don't apply) and no natural sequential structure (so NLP-style models don't apply), plus datasets are often small/medium (10k–100k rows) and features are heterogeneous (numeric + categorical). Tree ensembles thrive because they capture nonlinear interactions easily — "for free" through their splits, and gradient boosting consistently wins honest benchmarks on this kind of data.

Why tabular is different — and the verdict

Tabular data is heterogeneous (numeric + categorical), often small, and has strong classical baselines. The honest-benchmark verdict: gradient boosting usually wins. MLPs (numeric → normalized, categorical → embeddings/one-hot) are a simple, strong baseline but underperform GBMs.

Tabular-specific architectures

TabNet (attention + sparsity), FT-Transformer (feature-wise attention), SAINT (row + column attention), NODE / DeepGBM (hybrids). The shared idea: treat each feature as a "token" and use self-attention to learn feature interactions explicitly — the thing trees get for free. Still, mostly "no(t really)."

Drill the why

Why does "treat each feature as a token + self-attention" not automatically win? Self-attention is a powerful but data-hungry way to learn interactions, and it has no built-in inductive bias for tabular structure. Trees encode interactions through axis-aligned splits with almost no data cost. On the small/medium datasets typical of tabular problems, the net has to learn what a tree assumes — so it usually loses.

Why it matters — the practical takeaway

For structured/tabular problems, gradient-boosted trees remain the default. Don't reach for a deep net out of fashion. Cautionary literature (Makridakis 2018; Dacrema 2019 "Are we really making much progress?"; the "Transformers Are What You Don't Need" repo) warns that flashy neural results often don't survive honest re-evaluation.

Prior-Data Fitted Networks — TabPFN

The emerging exception. TabPFN = Tabular Prior-Data Fitted Network: a Transformer trained once on millions of synthetic tabular tasks, then applied directly to new datasets — a foundation model for tables.

  • Learns to do Bayesian inference over tasks.
  • At inference you feed in your small dataset in-context — no training per dataset.
  • No hyperparameter tuning.
  • Approximates Bayesian model averaging over many possible tabular models.

Status: v1 underperformed, but v2 / v2.5 are often on par with or better than GBM-based models. Datasets larger than 10k rows are a bit trickier but doable. (Repo: PriorLabs/TabPFN.)

ELI-intern — what makes TabPFN weird

Normal ML: train a fresh model on your data. TabPFN: the model is already trained — on millions of fake datasets — and you just show it your data as a prompt (in-context), like asking a language model a question. It outputs predictions in one forward pass, with zero tuning. Conceptually it's averaging over "every tabular model that could plausibly explain your data," which is why it approximates Bayesian model averaging.

16

Why does gradient boosting tend to beat deep nets on tabular data?

  1. Gradient boosting has far more parameters than any neural net.
  2. Tabular data lacks spatial/sequential structure and is often small, while trees capture interactions cheaply.
  3. Trees are the only models that cannot represent nonlinear interactions, which avoids overfitting.
17

What is genuinely novel about how TabPFN is used at prediction time?

  1. It trains a fresh deep net on your dataset with heavy hyperparameter tuning.
  2. It draws many bootstrap samples of your data and averages decision trees.
  3. It is pretrained once; you feed your data in-context with no training and no tuning.
